Proclamation
on anniversary of the
revolution of Lares : 1935
To Lares, to the holy land of the Homeland, that guards the sacred
ashes of the founders of the Republic, the nation convenes to
render a homage and to receive the inspiration of the valor and the sacrifice
that the heroes of the Revolution embodied.
The independence of the Homeland and its personality as a free
and sovereign nation was written with the noblest blood of all
generations.
The National Pilgrimage is directed to Lares to rescue the
will and the valor that we must emulate to restore the
Republic.
September 23 is the day of the heroes and martyrs of inde-
pendence. Every Puerto Rican vibrates before the evocation of the he-
roic ephemeris, will feel free from foreign interventions, and re-
new, the vow to cast from the native soil the enemy power that
attempts to hold national sovereignty.
Lares is the consecration of the nation to the armed revolution, the
fixed trajectory of all free peoples.
The present generations must write their heroic page
in the history of the Homeland. The manes of the liberators rise in
Lares to offer us the liberty and the sword to reconquer it.
Given in San Juan de Puerto Rico, on the day nineteen of
September of 1935, sixty-eighth year of the proclamation of the
Republic.
Nationalist Party of Puerto Rico
The National Board:
P. ALBIZU CAMPOS
President
LUIS F. VELAZQUEZ
General Secretary
El Mundo, September 20, 1935, p. 2.

Albizu Campos delivers the speech of the Day of the Discovery of Puerto Rico in the
Teatro Municipal (today Teatro Tapia) in San Juan on November 19, 1935.

Concept of the Race
By Pedro Albizu Campos
When the President of the Republic of La Plata, Don Hipólito
Yrigoyen, instituted the Day of the Race, to what race did the high
Argentine magistrate refer? Did his concept corner, with an ancient
predilection, the rest of the great ethnic mosaic of America? Did
the Argentine president refer to the Ibero-American race.
For us the race has nothing to do with biology. Neither pale complexion, nor frizzy hair, nor obliqueness of eye. Race is a perpe-
tuity of virtues and of characteristic institutions. We distinguish our-
selves by our culture, by our valor, by our nobility,
by our Catholic sense of civilization. When the
Spanish unity was realized in the union of the Aragonese and Castilian kingdoms and
the independence was conquered with the expulsion of the Saracens,
Spain, full of itself launched itself into the epic of the Discove-
ry, the greatest epic of contemporaneity. The Discove-
ry of man by man. God knows what moral cataclysm
made possible the separation of humanity to the degree of
not recognizing itself! With the Discovery Spain conquered
human unity, gave the world its geographic unity, made the
earth a sphere. Man did not know where he lived. His residence
plane was a conjecture. Spain gave him the security of stability and
upon feeling firm man began again to walk. The discove-
ry opened the path for all contemporary investigation
in the terrain of the philosophical and natural sciences. Not only did it capture
in system the science of oceanic navigation but also brought to
man the conscience of the cosmos, put man to navigate in an
immense fleet of constellations through infinite space. Will
the cosmos be infinite in the eternal perspective of that infinite space or
will its destiny be a perpetual heaping and unheaping in the
miracle of creation?

Palabra, October 19, 1935, p. 7

The primacy of the Spanish race in Europe proved the benefit
that an equilibrium of valor and of sanctity can give to the world. There
is a historic experience that destines us to search for ourselves
selves to return equilibrium to the world. Today the racial sense
resurges victorious. The yellows are grouped in the Orient to save
themselves. Japan feels providentially destined to
remake in Asia the yellow unity. The Ethiopian races react
equally in Africa. The Anglo-Saxons reunite rapidly
facing Latinity. It is a secret law of North American politics
to follow the inertia of the politics of its mother Homeland Great Britain.
Everyone has from us their spiritual arms Because we
were the ones who, in blood and fire, with the cross of the sword and with the sword of the cross gave to the old Europe and to the virgin America the
tradition of virtue, of valor, of honor, of sacrifice, of des-
regard for death and for material goods, that make of our
race today the only hope of the world.
In these moments in which Europe trembles before the
perspective of a bellicose catastrophe, in the hands of two men
of our race rests the peace of the Universe. Full faith in the values
of our race the world has put its fate in the hands of two
men of ours. Don Salvador de Madariaga y Linazo of Spain
and of Argentina, respectively, face, cold, serene,
right in responsibility, with the problem of peace, the ideal of
our race hoisted in the flag of Christianity with which our
friars brought civilization to half the planet and our heroes
gave the turn to the world.
What dangers are there? For our Race danger has never
existed.

Mourning of the victims of the
Massacre of Río Piedras
Over a promontory of humid earth, they bid farewell to the mourning,
pronouncing eloquent discourses the gentlemen Ermelindo Santia-
go, who spoke in the name of the patriotic Lodge, to which belonged the
gentleman Ramón S. Pagán; Carrera, as president of the National Federation
of Students, and the President of the Nationalist party,
Licensed Pedro Albizu Campos.
Mr. Albizu Campos said, among other things, that there
was "represented the entirety of Puerto Rico, from Cabo Rojo
to Vieques; from west to east and from north to south, sharing all
the immense mourning of liberty." Then he said that there had already been received
adhesions of diverse nations to the mourning; that "the yan-
kee enemy wants the blood of the Puerto Ricans to run"; that "today they
will avail themselves of a side to realize their purposes and tomorrow they will avail
themselves of another". "From the tomb of liberty -he said- I ask that Be-
nigno Fernández García, the Attorney General, bring us the accus-
sations against those policemen that assassinated Pagán and his compa-
ñeros, and assassinated José Santiago, when he had already surrendered,
when he was disarmed," He said that Colonel Riggs sent the
uniformed forces to Río Piedras "with the deliberate purpose of
assassinating the nationalist representation of Puerto Rico". He accused in
equal manner the governor Winship, and the Chancellor Chardón of
"making fun of the student body".
