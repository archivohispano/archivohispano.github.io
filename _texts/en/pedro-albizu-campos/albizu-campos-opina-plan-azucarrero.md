For example, the banana lands are in the hands of the Guánica, the Aguirre, the Fajardo, and some small native centrals, and in order to transfer them to the owners of marginal lands suppressed for sugarcane cultivation, it will be necessary to proceed with forced expropriation, and for this, legislation is required which will be a reality if the President of the United States so desires it in his day and in his hour.

The worker, as always, is used as a decoy to gain a favorable opinion.

The only thing that can save the worker from capitalist exploitation is the implementation of a respectable minimum wage and fewer hours of work by virtue of a law, but the United States is a nation so backward in matters of social legislation that a law of that nature is unconstitutional. The only thing that can save the Yankee worker from capitalist exploitation is revolution or an amendment to the current constitution of the United States.

Another idea that is at the mercy of the President of the United States is the source of money for the development of any plan. According to the Costigan-Jones law, the Yankee President can do whatever he wants with the money from the tax on sugar processing.

Nothing is more illusory than submitting to the will of a foreign leader nothing less than the rehabilitation of an entire nation.

The Yankee rehabilitation plan is only a continuation of the system of displacement implemented by their regime upon establishing itself in Puerto Rico. Native colonists will be denied refaction by the centrals and by the banks, and they will die. The life of the native centrals depends exclusively on the credit granted to them by the banks, and these will strictly comply with the orders they receive from the government of Washington in the sense of eliminating them one by one.

Let us remember what Mr. Roosevelt said: "the economic rehabilitation of Puerto Rico depends on the economic rehabilitation of the United States," according to him that means that the economic rehabilitation of the United States must be attended to first, and when that is achieved, one can imagine by appreciating the following facts: that there are more than ten million unemployed men in the United States; that there are more than forty million indigent people living on public charity; that the deficit of the North American treasury this year alone amounts to the respectable sum

of four billion dollars and is increasing; that the public debt amounts to twenty-seven billion dollars and no one knows how it will be paid; that the public power cannot contain the growing social revolution and is resorting to martial law throughout its territory to dominate the labor rebellion that wants to break the chains of misery.

Let us not be fools. If Mr. Roosevelt and the Congress of the United States have not been able to put their house in order; if all representative Yankee men feel that the institutions of their country are faltering, threatened today with ruin by a conflagration that is believed inevitable, how is it possible to expect anything good from such sources and how are they going to take care of our problems?

Puerto Rico will solve its problems with its own political independence by virtue of which our country will have dominion over its customs, and thus will be able to promote its own industry and acquire markets abroad for its products in fair reciprocity.

Currently, by virtue of the present regime, our merchandise is treated as Yankee products and not admissible in foreign markets, except under strong restrictions imposed in retaliation for the high tariffs prevailing in North America.

To worsen the situation, the Yankee rehabilitation plan imposed on Puerto Rico deprives us of the American market; and our products that the Yankees need, they pay for at the price they please, and in view of their commercial monopoly of our market, they sell to us at the price that seems to them.

We believe that the hour has arrived for all Puerto Rican interests to unite in a single bundle to the nationalism of Puerto Rico so that they can save their own existence, even if they have no patriotism.

Proclamation on the anniversary of the Lares revolution
of 1934

The governmental political parties have dissolved. They
were constituted to govern and have disappeared because under a
foreign intervention the nation cannot confer powers to its
own citizens because the intervener has usurped them.

The illusion of elections to reach power has vanished.

In the electoral struggle of the year 1932, the coalition of republicans and socialists triumphed in the elections. The scrutiny awarded it more than two hundred thousand votes. A result that represents the absolute majority of the votes.

The coalition took charge of the Insular legislature to discover that there is no such legislature, because it is at the mercy of the absolute veto of the president of the United States; that all the laws of the country can be annulled motu proprio by the congress of the United States; and that Yankee foreign tribunals fix its interpretation. It aspired to executive positions, and found that it was also subject to the whim of the imperial chief or his mandataries.

The directors of the Coalition aspired to enjoy at least the subordinate positions of the budget, but with the greatest astonishment, they have seen the liberals, their governmental opponents defeated in 1932, called by the Yankee power to take charge of good executive positions and especially of the Emergency Administration, called the Rehabilitadora, which has the only solvent fund at this time.

The coalicionistas could not explain this anomaly. What happens is that to enjoy the "budgetary good" it is not necessary to win elections. The Yankee intervening power will always divide that privilege among all the governmental political parties, or

better said, among the men who swear loyalty and cooperation to it, because, not even in the selection for important positions that it must award to a certain government party, will the Yankee power take into consideration the directing bodies of the same party that appears benefited.

The nation is wanted to be anarchized with the government parties, and these, ignoring the authority of their own directors.

The Coalition goes through the experience of the old unionism, when despite its copious electorates, it had to bend to the will of the foreign power that imposed in many cases the hegemony of its republican opponents, defeated in all the elections.

The intervening power will seek its instruments where it finds them, at its discretion, to ensure the development of its exploiting and absorbing policy, and to reduce to absolute political and economic impotence the natives of the country.

The remedy lies in patriotically refusing all cooperation with the regime. Empires exist if the citizens of the intervened nations offer themselves as administrative troops to crush their own countrymen.

Any entity that cannot fulfill any purpose is dissolved. Such is the situation of all government parties, whether they are called liberals, republicans, or socialists. None has been able to fulfill the most innocent of their postulates after having won an election. In the "triumph," they have had to dedicate themselves to the shameful task of offering their best men to serve in subordinate positions under the supreme direction of the Yankee "experts," and thus, complete the demoralizing work that the enemy sees necessary to carry out to prevent the uprising of the national spirit that ends with the foreign domination.

The Coalition, dissolved as it is, harbors no hope in its deep deception; and the liberals, aware, see their party in ruins, when it serves only as a guide at the doors of the Capitolio, holding out a whole hand to receive the piece of meat or the crackers of the "Rehabilitadora."

And that deception of the liberals has to be well felt in the noble elements that remain within their bosom, because a grouping that aspired to represent the Homeland, has become a defender of the policy of Mr. Roosevelt, who pretends that this

great nation, of wealth and legendary dignity, passively admit before history, having become the first country of beggars that has passed through the earth.

The economic interests that had been handed over for so many years to those governmental political parties, seeing their impotence to resolve anything, have dispensed with them and expect everything from Washington. The proletarian people, whose virile fiber has been weakened by government propaganda, deposit their dearest hopes in the crackers and pork of the "Rehabilitadora".

The dissolution of those government parties is so complete, that their directors of greater responsibility, remain silent or withdraw, to give way to figures of the second order who have the audacity to work with letters of mendicancy, digging for themselves the grave of contempt of the noblest of the nationality.

The country has seen clearly at last, that, under the Yankee intervention it does not count with any power acquirable by suffrage, and it goes to Washington. Not to the Congress of the United States, because everyone knows that the Congress has time to take care of the affairs of Puerto Rico when it suits it to sink our interests. Neither do they resort to President Roosevelt, because this gentleman is exclusively interested in Puerto Rico for him and his own to stroll around, and after he is in Washington he orders his lackeys to conform with a cup of tea the cheap politicians of Puerto Rico who intend to bother him in his serious work.

The commissions are forcibly directed to irresponsible subordinates, accompanied by our coryphaei, megaphones of the gross economic imperialism that squeezes us, who are in charge of keeping alive the creole imagination with the eternal dream of a dance of millions, in whose dream all the unfortunate unwary must die of hunger and exhaustion.

The descending order that the Yankee interventionist regime traces is proprietor, peon and beggar.

And this order is a patriotic duty to reverse, but, for this it is necessary to suppress all foreign intervention.
