UNITED STATES HAS A DUTY TO FULFILL
WITH US
AND IT IS TO WITHDRAW FROM OUR COUNTRY - IT IS THE
ONLY WAY TO DEMONSTRATE RESPECT TO THE
LAW OF NATIONS - ALBIZU CAMPOS 70
On Monday morning we visited Dr. Pedro Albizu
Campos, the supreme leader, in his office at the central offices of the Nationalist Party, who at that time was very busy
answering the numerous correspondence from home and abroad
coming from countless organizations, co-religionists and friends
inside and outside the nation.
Upon noticing our presence in his office, he immediately
extended his firm right hand and with his proverbial smile inquired: "what
news is there?" "None, Don Pedro," we replied, "other than
what you can provide us. Do not forget that the readers of LA
NACION anxiously await your announced declarations."
-Ask, then - he replied.
-What do you tell us about the current political moment?
-"I already had the opportunity to officially present from the
nationalist tribune in Guayama our attitude in relation to the present
political panorama of the country.
-"Since elsewhere in this edition we publish the
review of the act of Guayama we immediately asked another
question.
-How will the United States Congress consider the project of
the elective governor of Mr. La Guardia, Democratic Representative for
New York in that legislative body?
-The nationalist movement is not interested in the consideration that
the United States Congress gives to any project submitted to
that legislative body. We maintain that the Puerto Rican nation is
absolute arbiter of its destiny, that the Treaty of Paris imposed
by the United States to Spain, in what refers to Puerto Rico, is null
because our country was not a contracting party in said treaty; nor did Spain have
powers to cede Puerto Rico nor the United States to accept it in
cession.
By virtue of the Autonomic Charter, which constituted a treaty between
the Mother Homeland and the island of Puerto Rico, our sovereignty was
internationally recognized and it was the duty of every nation

to respect it, starting with the United States.
When Nationalism rises energetically for the vindication
of its sovereignty, the invading power tries to disorient public
opinion, to protect its usurpation and its dispossession by imposing
concessions to the oppressed country that perpetuate the foreign oppressor
regime.
Liberalism has its insolences. Let us not forget, the pretentious
liberals of all empires; they have always been imperialists who have
suggested the most subtle means to complete foreign absorption.
It is preferable for a nationality, victim of a military occupation,
a frank and decided despotism that serves as a spur and that awakens indigenous resistance, to a veiled and liberal despotism that anesthetizes national
consciousness.
The United States has a duty to fulfill with us. And it is
to withdraw immediately from our country. It is the only way to
demonstrate respect to the law of nations.
Immense damage has been caused by that invading nation to our
nationality, by depriving it of the exercise of its sovereignty and submitting it to the
most barbaric and inhumane economic exploitation known to history.
If any North American legislator wants to do some good to his
Homeland, he must propose respect for the rights of others. If the representative
La Guardia wants us to deal with his projects, let him propose
to his country the immediate withdrawal of the North American armed forces
from our land, and the payment of reparations that they owe us for the immense damage caused in our country during 33 years of North American
military occupation.
"The apparently liberal manifestations of Mr. La Guardia
are directed especially to the Puerto Rican colony of New York
to obtain from it the votes that Mr. La Guardia needs to
sustain himself in power.
"The National Board has given instructions to the New York Board
to inform our compatriots in that modern Babel that they refrain
from the internal struggles in that country so that they do not allow their feelings to be exploited by any North American politician; that their only
duty is to organize themselves to restore the Republic of their Homeland.
-What significance do you attribute to the revolutionary movement
of the Philippines?
Fearing the Philippine revolution, the North American government by Law
of 1917 committed itself to recognizing the Independence of the Philippine Islands
(as soon as there was a stable government) and in the opinion of
the government of the United States, there has not yet been "a stable government"
in the Philippines nor will there ever be despite the fact that the Philippine government is of North American
making.
"The Filipinos have been waiting for this promise for fourteen years."
"In that period, North American interests have been strengthening.
The conservative sentiment has been stimulated in
important nationalist sectors of that archipelago to such a point that
the eminent statesman Roxas more or less ten months ago, has raised
the flag of non-cooperation adopting tactics very similar to those

of Puerto Rican nationalism.
"The Filipinos clearly see that the moment is most opportune for the
immediate suppression of the North American military occupation. The United
States does not count today with a friendly nation in the entire Universe,
because its policy of absorption has been unmasked. The North American
power is going through an internal crisis of revolutionary character produced by the economic crisis, a consequence of the
loss of markets.
"I firmly believe that the Filipinos will act to impose their
independence.
"The opportunity that the Puerto Rican nation has is identical to that
of the Philippine nation. Our duty is to act immediately.

RADIO CORPORATION OF PORTO RICO' SUBSIDIARY
OF THE INTERNATIONAL TELEGRAPH & TELEPHONE CO.,
REFUSES TO TRANSMIT POLITICAL SPEECHES

CENSORSHIP ON NATIONALISM PROPAGANDA.
STATEMENTS BY THE PRESIDENT OF THE PARTY DON
PEDRO ALBIZU CAMPOS 71

"For some time now, influence has been exerted on the company
"Radio Corporation of Porto Rico" subsidiary of the "International
Telegraph and Telephone Co." to suspend the
transmission by radio of nationalist speeches.
It is absolutely certain that the North American government wanted
to suppress our propaganda by air and it is also
absolutely certain that the pro-Yankee elements yearned for the same
purpose.
We must call attention to the fact that in the United States there is no
freedom of speech by air. What has been
denominated indirect censorship prevails there, which is a formal admission that there is no freedom of speech, nor of thought, in that country. In the Yankee
Congress a bill is being considered to suppress
those restrictions of thought and of right.
Under a regime of force such as the one that prevails in Puerto Rico, under
this North American military occupation, there is no right and yes
a mere tolerance in the emission of thought, because the North American
power trusts in its strength and in the dominion of the press.
Propaganda by wireless had national and
international effects and has forced the invader to unmask himself, imposing
a censorship.
The excuse given by the "Radio Corporation of Porto Rico", subsidiary
of the "International Telegraph and Telephone Co." is trivial. To allege
that a transmission of thought so vital for Puerto Rico is suppressed,
to dedicate its apparatus to commercial announcements and to news of the
races of the Hippodrome, is to confess that they are acting under the
coercion of the North American government and in agreement with the oppressor
policy of that government, especially when those radio transmissions represented a very substantial income for the company.
They intend to besiege us by air since we are by sea and land.

Here are published the cables that do not harm the
North American imperialist policy and the information agencies Associated Press
and United Press transmit news that is of interest to that government.
The "International Telegraph and Telephone Co." has incurred in a
very serious responsibility by lending itself to this maneuver of the North American government
and we must demand the corresponding responsibility.
The same haste that the head of the North American
occupation, Colonel Roosevelt, has had to deny statements not made until the moment, on the interference of the North American
government in this serious matter, confirms that interference.
The country can raise a transmitting station if it so wishes. And
it must do so immediately, to put an end to the monopoly that the foreign
company "International Telegraph and Telephone Co." enjoys.

It is true that permission is needed to transmit, which could be
denied by the North American occupying power. It is worth, however,
forcing the invader to take off his mask completely.
If that permission were denied, it must be dispensed with. The Puerto Rican nation must not tolerate being besieged by air.
The "Radio Corporation of Porto Rico", subsidiary of the
"International Telegraph and Telephone Co." and denominated Station
W.K.A.Q., has declared itself an enemy of our sacred rights. The
entire nation has to demand responsibilities from this local corporation
and from the "International Telegraph and Telephone Co.," of which it is
subsidiary and which without a doubt has sanctioned that attack against our
sovereignty.

LETTER TO MR. LUIS SANCHEZ MORALES 72

July 1, 1932.

Hon. Luis Sánchez Morales,
President of the Senate and to the
Senators.

Gentlemen:

In our memorial filed before the legislature at the opening of
the present extraordinary session, by virtue of a mandate from the National
Board of the Nationalist Party of Puerto Rico, we claim for
our patriotic organization equality before the electoral law. The Press
of today informs us that that body has approved in third reading the
electoral bill submitted to its consideration.
Nationalism was not granted the opportunity to be heard despite
the terms of its memorial already cited. The project has been approved
unanimously although it does not take into consideration the situation of the
Nationalist party.
This action of the Senate is simultaneous with the letter from the Executive
Secretary communicating to us the suspension of the registration of the new
candidacies that we have filed in his secretariat, pretexting the wait
for a new opinion of the attorney general that will illustrate him on the
right to register candidacies by convention, when by right already
recognized, the Nationalist party has filed general candidacies.
We see in all this machinations of the intervening governor to
prevent Nationalism from being able to go to the polls. What happened in the year 1924 is repeated. It is hard for us to believe that that body without
distinction of parties has entered into an anti-patriotic understanding
with the North American power, to prevent the nationalist will of the
country from manifesting itself peacefully in the electoral contests.
Given the gravity of the situation that that policy could bring if it
proves to be true, it is our duty to claim again, equality
before the electoral law, and, to that effect, we request that we be granted to be
heard.

With all consideration
P. ALBIZU CAMPOS
President of the Nationalist Party

Albizu Campos is acclaimed as he makes his
entrance to the people's theater of Lares on
September 23, 1931.

NATIONALISM AND THE ELECTORAL SITUATION 73

Messrs. D. Luis Sánchez Morales and
D. Manuel F. Rossy, presidents of the
Senate and the Chamber respectively.

Gentlemen:

In compliance with the mandate that has been imposed on me by resolution
the National Board of the Nationalist Party of Puerto Rico, I have the
honor to address you this communication to claim equality before the
electoral law to which our patriotic organization has the right.

Nationalism has systematically refrained from entering into the
bitter controversy over the electoral law because that fratricidal struggle
benefits exclusively the North American intervener who
contemplates it smiling and with approval. Nationalism will always compromise
with its Puerto Rican countrymen before favoring in any way, the policy of the Yankee foreign intervention that intends
to annihilate Puerto Rico.

We do not believe in the tactics of the parties with whom we painfully
have to face, because those tactics are condemned by the very
experience of those same groups, which have exhausted their patience
in a sterile diplomacy for the Government of the United States
to respect our sovereignty.

Our country is in ruins. It has lost in thirty-two years more than
seventy percent of its wealth that has passed to Yankee interests
by virtue of the monopoly they have of our market, which obliges us
to buy theirs under the conditions they want to impose on us. And to
sell them ours under the terms that are pleasing to them.

We call attention to the fact that the high purchasing power that
Puerto Rico accuses and that allows it to occupy the position of the fifth
market for the United States in the world, and the first in Latin America, is not based on real income, but on the displacement of our farmers, industrialists and merchants, who are buying

not with profits derived from capital, but with the remainder of those
capitals after their forced liquidation by virtue of the public
auction of the goods to satisfy the contributory burden that falls
only on the natives of the country, as is proper in every
regime of foreign intervention.

That system does not respect even the most humble home, and day after day
we have to contemplate the effect of that silent war of the
public auction, which throws to the side of the roads the entire Puerto Rican family. No well-intentioned government would allow such a
situation, because the contributory power can never be exercised for the
extermination of the ancestral home, as happens as happens in Puerto
Rico; the exercise of that power for the promotion of the economic
independence of the home is permissible.

No people can stand idly by to see the proletarian tide rise
that ascends with indifference, conspiracy or cowardice
characteristic.

The social and cultural ills inflicted on the Puerto Rican
nation during the Yankee intervention are too clear to require
more analysis.

That is why, and based on the very tragic experience of our
countrymen who do not militate in our ranks, that nationalism has emerged
to put a definitive stop to the Yankee government that sucks the blood of the
Homeland. Our program is clear as soon as we receive the suffrage
of the Puerto Rican majorities will be immediately declared the
celebration of the Constituent Convention of the Republic.

We do not recognize the validity of the Yankee regime, because it is based on
the nullity of the Treaty of Paris. On the date of its signing by the Mother
Homeland Spain, Puerto Rico had international personality, and no
treaty of Spain could bind us without the express consent of the
Insular Parliament, and that indispensable requirement for its validity was not
complied with. In other words, Puerto Rico was not a tradable thing and
could not be subject to traffic, not only by virtue of the principles of
natural law, but by mandate of the strictest positive law
that informs the life of relationship between civilized nations.

We resort to peaceful means to effect the change of
regime because it is a norm that must be respected in the contemporary
age. Modern empires are obliged to respect the
independence of the intervened countries when the national will
is shown to be unwavering. Empires consult their convenience, and this
is never found in provoking the revolution that puts an end to their
interests. It has been ten years since the Nationalist Party was constituted. It has
resorted to the elections of the year 1924, in some ten municipalities and
the year 1928 only in Caguas, under the present current electoral law,
made under the direction of the head of the intervention in the year 1924, Mr.
Towner, and approved unanimously at the suggestions of the same

governor. The purpose was to prevent any
nationalist sentiment from manifesting itself in the country.
Currently the Party has registered general candidacies by
convention, but, according to the current interpretation, it does not have
representation in the Insular Board of Elections, and in the polling stations
it will have only an observer with voice but without vote, if the
current law is respected, which is being interpreted by the Superintendent of
Elections, Mr. Terry, as is natural in a North American official, in the most restrictive sense in what concerns the vital interests of the Nationalist Party.

We have been contemplating the electoral picture with patience waiting
for this legislative assembly. The head of the occupation, as befits the one
in command, has submitted to you the "law" project that does not take into consideration, ex professo, as was to be expected the position of party by convention to which nationalism has arrived.

We are aware that if you do not accept the will already expressed
by the Governor, the tacit will of the Yankee Congress is expected
that defines in advance who should triumph in the elections. You do not have
legislative powers because you stumble with the absolute veto or with the
annulment of your acts by the Yankee Congress. Yours is, without
limitations, however, the legislative initiative, and it is to be expected that,
as Puerto Ricans, you accept only a law that guarantees the
patriotic cause of nationalism that does not dispute anything to you unless
it is the sacrifice to constitute a free Homeland for all
Puerto Ricans.

In international life, nationalism is representative; internally, it is
the goad that obliges the invader to cede the fruit that does not fall into
our hands, but into the lap of the parties that represent the middle way.
Nationalism is the only thing that belongs to every Puerto Rican.

It claims from you the respect to your most sacred right: the
independence of the Homeland.
With all consideration,

P. ALBIZU CAMPOS
President,
Nationalist Party
