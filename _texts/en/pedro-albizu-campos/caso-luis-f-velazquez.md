The case of
Luis F. Velázquez

Introduction
On June 14, 1932, an event was held at the Municipal Theater of
San Juan (now Teatro Tapia) in homage to the North
American flag. The governor, James R. Beverly, and the
Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of Puerto Rico, Emilio del
Toro y Cuebus, participated. Mr. del Toro was, in turn, the President of the
Celebration Committee of the Bicentennial of the Birth of George
Washington.
Luis F. Velázquez, a member of the Nationalist Party, went
the next day, June 15, to the office of Judge Emilio del
Toro. He arrived in front of the Judge and said: "The acts such as the one
last night that have been perpetuated in the country and that you so conspicuously
sponsor, constitute an open challenge to our Homeland; and
that offense that you inflict on the country I have taken up to return
it to you for being a traitor and a scoundrel". Immediately
he approached him and slapped him in the face.
For this act, Mr. Velázquez was accused in the Municipal Court
(now District Court) for the crime of attacking and assaulting
a public official in the performance of his duties. He was decla-
red guilty and sentenced to one year in prison.
Not satisfied with his conviction, Velázquez, through his
attorney Lcdo. Albizu Campos, requested and obtained a trial de Novo
before the District Court (now Superior Court), San Juan Division.
He was tried and convicted of the same crime and sentenced
to the same penalty. Velázquez then appealed to the Supreme Court of
Puerto Rico.

In the Supreme Court, Chief Justice del Toro,
recused himself. It was up to the four associate justices to resolve the case:
the Americans Adolph G. Wolf and Harvey M. Hutchinson, and
the Puerto Ricans Félix Córdova Dávila and Pedro de Aldrey.

The appellant Velázquez alleged that the events occurred in the
Office of Judge del Toro in the Supreme Court of Puerto Rico,
then located on the second floor of the Cuartel de los Dominicos
(known as the Convento de los Dominicos). This building had
become the property of the United States government, so
the courts of Puerto Rico lacked jurisdiction to judge him.
His argument was rejected.
In an opinion signed by Judge Wolf, the Supreme Court of
Puerto Rico affirmed the appealed sentence. (See: Pueblo v.
Velázquez, 45 D.P.R. 905, 1933).
The accused appealed, through his attorney Lcdo. Albizu
Campos, to the United States Court of Appeals
for the First Circuit, located in Boston. This Court reversed the
Supreme Court and ordered the filing of the complaint against Ve-
lázquez. (See: People v. Velázquez. 77 F 2nd. 431, 1935, ехре-
diente número 2923).
The Court concluded that, effectively, the government of Puerto
Rico lacked jurisdiction over the properties transferred to the
government of the United States.
It also noted that it constituted an act of mere tolerance on
the part of the United States government that the military
authorities allowed the Supreme Court of Puerto Rico to occupy part
of the building of the Cuartel de los Dominicos, which did not confer juris-
diction to the government of Puerto Rico.
The government of Puerto Rico appealed to the Supreme
Court of the United States but it refused to review the case.
(See: People v. Velázquez, 296 U.S. 602, 1935).
Here we have included part of the transcription of the evidence
of the case; an allegation of assignment of errors; a motion on
lack of jurisdiction; and the allegation submitted by Albizu Campos to the
Court of Appeals in Boston.

ALLEGATION

To the Honorable Tribunal:
The accused through his attorney who subscribes respectfully submits to the consideration of the Honorable Tribunal the
following allegation:
ERROR ASSIGNED
The lower court erred fundamentally:
1. In holding that the accused had committed the crime of
Assault and Battery;
2. In holding that he had committed that crime with aggravating
circumstances;
3. In imposing on the accused, in the event that he had committed the
crime imputed to him an unusual penalty;
4. In sustaining the objection of the Prosecutor to prevent the
accused in his direct testimony to answer this question: "Do you
know what happened to Mr. del Toro for that propaganda in the
University of Puerto Rico with the students?" (p. 23 of the
record);
5. In sustaining the objection of the Prosecutor to prevent the
accused in his direct testimony to answer this question: "Do you know
if that act sponsored by Mr. del Toro was censured by the
press adverse to nationalism?" (p. 23 of the record);
6. In declaring inadmissible a number of the edition of the newspaper
La Correspondencia, of June 17, 1932, non-nationalist newspaper
that is published in San Juan de Puerto Rico and that contains an article
entitled "The homage to the flag in the municipal theater", by
Mr. M. Alfaro;

7. In declaring inadmissible the editorial of La Linterna,
non-nationalist weekly that is published in San Juan de Puerto Rico
(p. 25 of the record).

CIRCUMSTANCES
The parties involved in this case represent the
extremes in matters of political opinion in Puerto Rico. Mr.
Velázquez, the accused, is a radical separatist, Mr. del Toro is
an annexationist, devoted to the cause that he believes is good.
This court is aware of the political environment that
prevails in a nation intervened like ours by a foreign power, and is aware of the special
political environment in which we are debated.
In the serene and just appreciation of that situation that worries everyone
deeply because it entails nothing less than the definitive
status of our country, lies the justice of the ruling that must fall
in this case.
That serenity is invoked from a court whose President is the
aggrieved party, and, in which two natives of the United States of North America sit as magistrates, and, it is invoked, to the
gentlemen judges, as judges and gentlemen, an accused, who is a consecrated separatist, and has obeyed the noblest and most
respectable impulse, which is patriotism.
Appears before you, this gentleman accused before the
judicial power created by the North American intervening nation, in
serene expectation of the justice that must emanate from judges and gentlemen
representatives of the nation that the accused marks as enemies of his
homeland.
We are before history and before posterity. The sentence that
falls in this case will have a transcendent significance. It is about
the clash between the principle of our own national determination
and the expansionism of a foreign power that invades us.
The penal sanction is necessary to maintain the peace of an
organized society, when the individual, acting for antisocial
motives, attacks and attacks those who with him are engaged in the
conservation of that social unit to whose existence all are
consecrated.

It is necessarily inapplicable to this state the case of Puerto
Rico, in which the Puerto Rican society, with its atavisms, tradi-

tion, language, and its own Christian civilization struggles for its existence
against an assimilation of the foreign that however estima-
ble it may be, it is foreign, and that has always been enough to
produce the most bloody clashes in history.

Not an aggressor is being judged who has been moved by material
interests, not even by a matter of personal honor. The
fiscal evidence confirms that it is an extraordinary motive:
to demand a reparation in the field of gentlemen for a grievance
that he believes perpetrated to the Puerto Rican dignity.

Nor is it a hallucinated person. Mr. Velázquez is a
prestigious gentleman who occupies a position of responsibility as
father of family, businessman and public man as
will be clearly seen in the record.

Nor is the grievance in vain. It is of public domain that a few days
before the date of the events, a magna
national pilgrimage in atonement to the national flag for
attempted attempt to convert it into a colonial flag was celebrated on the South Coast. It seemed to
everyone a reply to that solemn act, the homage, immediately
after, to the flag of the United States of North America celebrated
in the Municipal theater of San Juan, act sponsored by Mr.
del Toro. Even the non-nationalist press harshly censured that
ceremony. It was considered an imposition that had to be disdained.
(La Correspondencia and La Linterna, editions that formed part
of the record). It resulted in a tarnished act, because not even the North Americans residents attended it. (La Corresponden-
cia, id.).

Besides, Mr. del Toro confessed to having always sponsored
acts of that nature, although he could not understand why
they were repudiable by the majority of Puerto Ricans. (Decla-
ration of Mr. del Toro).

It is also illustrative that Mr. del Toro believes his homeland is
the United States of North America. (Declaration of Mr. del Toro).
CONSIDERATIONS OF THE ERRORS ASSIGNED
1. Absence of Mens Rea. From the evidence is detached the
absence of Mens Rea. To require a gentleman to offer repara-
tion in the field of gentlemen does not constitute public crime. The
declaration of Mr. del Toro clearly confirmed that that was all that
it was about. He confesses to have been slightly touched, and, that
although he, Mr. del Toro, assaulted Mr. Velázquez several times, he

did not answer and withdrew. There was no treachery, nor any of the
circumstances that nimbus the commission of a crime. Mediated a
card, with name, and address of Mr. Velázquez, which is what is
customary in cases of honor, so that the aggrieved person knows where to address.

The most demanding judicial power, and, even if it were illegitimate, cannot evade the consideration of the social norms that
prevail among gentlemen, in all circumstances, either to
exonerate or either to attenuate the penalty.

It was to be expected within the norms that prevail among
us, that Mr. del Toro would not allow that this matter
would even be treated in the courts. We have heard his point of
view when declaring, that he has done so, to comply with an order from
a court, but, we cannot understand him in this case.

The legislation covers only the situations in which the indi-
viduo wants to break the norms of the society in which he lives, but
does not pretend to cover those norms because it would be absolutely inne-
cessary.

We trust that the gentlemen magistrates will see this situa-
tion with all the objectivity that excludes the possibility of Mens Rea.
2. The statute by which the accused, Mr. Velázquez, has to be judged, is taken from the almost identical statute of Texas. The
jurisprudence of Texas establishes the requirements to fix the commission
of this crime:
We copy from the English Text:—
"To constitute an aggravated assault on an officer, it must appear
that the person assaulted was an officer in the discharge of his
duties and that the assault was made as an interruption of his
official duties."
Curlin v. State 209 S. W. 666.

"For an assault upon a person who is an officer to be aggravat-
ed assault, within Penal Code 1895 art 601, which provides that
when an assault is commited upon an officer in the lawful dis-
charge of his duties etc. it must appear that the person assaulted
was an officer in the actual discharge of his duties, and that such
assault was made as an interruption of his duties. Jeannes v. State
132 S.W. 352, 60 Texas Cr. 440.

From the evidence it is detached that Mr. Velázquez did not go to deal
with Mr. del Toro nothing concerning the charge of Mr. del Toro.

Mr. del Toro declares that he was resting, that that was the
time of his "lunch", what he had just done; that he was taking
a few walks; that to treat matters unrelated to his charge, such as those of the
Bicentennial of the Liberator of the United States, he received visits in his
office. Mr. del Toro was not fulfilling any duty
of his charge, nor could he be interrupted in the fulfillment of any duty.

The Prosecutor did not present a single official duty that
Mr. del Toro was fulfilling at that moment.

It seems to us that the own jurisprudence of this court is
definitive in excluding any possibility of aggravating circumstances.

3. The penalty of one year of reclusion in jail in a case like
this is unusual. Unusual penalties are repudiated because with them
goes the loss of confidence in the justice of the courts what
provokes the need to do justice by oneself. It is the method
most effective to restore violence on a large scale.

We have ransacked the jurisprudence, and we have not found
a penalty of that magnitude in a case of this nature.

If the judges are to show themselves not already unjust but inhuman,
they are inviting the commission of serious crimes, so that the accused
can find if not justice, at least humanity in a jury,
that reveals more sensitivity and respect for the feelings more
expensive for every gentleman.

4. In the direct examination of the accused Mr. Velázquez, the defense
asked him this question:

"Do you know what happened to Mr. del Toro for that propaganda
at the University of Puerto Rico with the students?"

The Judge, sustained the objection of the prosecutor, and prohibited that the
witness declare.

In the direct examination of the witness there was perfect right to
ask him that question, because Mr. del Toro had declared that he
considered his duty to sponsor acts like the one that provoked the
incident causing this accusation, and, it was necessary so that one
could understand the spirit of the accused, the reaction that a
similar act had provoked Mr. del Toro in the first
teaching center of the country. At least that fact, with all its amplitude,
would have mitigated the rigor of the penalty. The accused was deprived of that right.

"Since, however, Iglesias had previously been

"It is a matter of utmost importance to the defendant that he be
tried before the proper Court. It is also clearly the duty of

the Federal Court not to assume jurisdiction over this case unless
the facts, as they have been accepted, place the case
absolutely within the limitations of the Federal Court in
Puerto Rico. Mr. Muñoz Morales has presented a
capable and ingenious argument in defending his allegation that
Iglesias must be tried only by an Insular Court and that
the present indictment, brought by a Federal Grand Jury, must
be dismissed."

"It would be very pleasing to the one who subscribes to this opinion if he could
discern clearly to concur with the ideas so ingeniously
expressed by Mr. Muñoz Morales and suspend the case
from the calendar of this Court. It is not a pleasant task to preside over the trial
of any man accused of murder. I completely
agree with the views of Mr. Muñoz Morales that
there is no presumption in favor of the jurisdiction of this Court;
moreover in case of doubt this Court, must resolve it against
the presumed jurisdiction and not in favor of retaining it. Nothing have I read in
a long time that expressed my views in relation to
this matter so explicitly as the language used by the
Honorable David J. Brewer, who was Associate Justice of the
Supreme Court of the United States at the time of his death and who,
presiding as Circuit Judge in the year 1885, issued an
important opinion in the case of Kansas v. Bradley, published in
26 Fed. 289, in the final paragraph of his opinion, on page 292, states the following: "It must be borne in mind that when there is doubt
as to their jurisdiction, the Federal Courts must return the
case. They should not be greedy but rather detached in regard to
jurisdiction. The overburdened list of litigation of this Court should not
be crowded with cases brought unless their jurisdiction
is clear and the mandate of the law makes it imperative".

"Of course, that case was a civil litigation and involved a
matter of transfer and return, but the principle stated by
Judge Brewer should be followed more faithfully in a case where
a man's life is involved than in one where only his
property is involved. Therefore, it is my duty to dismiss this
charge and return the defendant Iglesias to the Insular Court unless
after careful study and determination, I am convinced
that only the Federal Court has jurisdiction to try him."

"The question of jurisdiction or the absence thereof in this

EIGHTH ERROR ASSIGNED
The Supreme Court erred in upholding the ruling of the Court of
District of San Juan, which held that the case was one of aggravated
assault.

The local statute establishing the crime of assault and
aggravated assault is an exact copy of the same statute of the state of
Texas.

As we had no precedents in Puerto Rico, we have had
to resort to those statutes of Texas.
In *Curlin v. State* 209 S. W. 666, the Court expressed:
"To constitute an aggravated assault, it must appear that
the person attacked was an officer in the performance of his duties
and that the assault was carried out with the intention of interrupting
such duties."

And in *Jeannes v. State*, 132 S.W. 352, 60 Texas, Cr. 440, the
Court expressed:
"In order for an assault against a person who is an officer
constitute aggravated assault, as provided in the Penal Code of
1895, Article 601, the assault must have been committed
against an officer who is fulfilling the legal duties of his
etc... It must appear that the person assaulted was an officer
in the actual performance of his duties and that said assault was
carried out as an interruption of his duties."

From the evidence it is clearly seen that the defendant did not go to
visit Mr. Toro for any matter affecting the
official duties of the latter.

Mr. del Toro himself testified that he was resting,
that he had just had lunch as usual, that he had
walked after lunch; that he could be seen in his office
for other matters not related to his official duties.
(Rec. p. 6-12).

The Prosecutor could not show a single official function in which
Mr. del Toro was engaged at the time of the alleged
aggression.
