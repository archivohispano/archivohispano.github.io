The nation stands tall and serenely contemplates those who, sheltered by force, have the audacity to proclaim themselves arbiters of its destinies. Mr. Roosevelt has been administering Puerto Rico for fifteen months. As was to be expected, the only thing he has done is to continue the policy traced by his predecessors to demolish the economy of Puerto Rico, and thus convert a great nation of free and happy citizens into a herd of peons, into a sugar batey at the mercy of Yankee overseers. Mr. Roosevelt has increased the consumption taxes paid by the people, in the respectable sum amounting to more than twenty-five million dollars annually, to enrich the industrialist and the farmer of his country.
Within the Yankee tariff fence, Puerto Rico has lost all its favorable trade balance, which has gone to the Yankee coffers, a sum amounting to some four hundred million dollars in thirty-four (34) years. Within the same regime of Yankee commercial monopoly, forced to sell at the price that the enemy imposes and to buy at the price that he dictates to us, conservative calculations yield a loss for Puerto Rico for the same period of time of some six hundred million dollars.
The Administration of Mr. Roosevelt has not deemed sufficient that tribute of one billion dollars during the presence of the Yankee military forces in Puerto Rico, and raises the tribute at a rate of some twenty-five million additional per year.
The United States feels its international isolation, the loss of its markets, and the worries of a social crisis.
The Yankee financial oligarchy had lost its popular prestige, and Mr. Roosevelt has restored it in a short time. The working masses, with twelve million men without work and without opportunity to organize themselves before the absolute power of capital, are in worse conditions than before he came to power, due to the rise in the prices of basic necessities without the corresponding rise in wages. Strikes are resolved in the traditional Yankee way, by machine-gunning the people; and before feeding the indigent, food is destroyed so that it rises in price, and the consumer will pay the capitalist the cost of the reduction. The ignorant Yankee masses are anesthetized with the mellifluous verb of the present occupant of the White House.

In foreign relations, there have not been those triumphs, despite the declarations of "good neighborliness". Yankee wants to consolidate what he has and has simulated with Cuba, Nicaragua and Haiti. His ambition always reveals his true intentions in Colombia, and his Press reveals them.
Mr. Roosevelt comes to Puerto Rico en route to Haiti, Colombia, and Panama. Here he will say some kind words for our America, its culture, and its civilization. He, like every enlightened North American, knows that Iberian America is one at its core, and that Puerto Rico is part of that great whole that is the object of North American greed.
In such circumstances, the announced visit of the President of the United States of North America is doubly unwelcome.

To alter the North American rehabilitation plan, implemented in Puerto Rico, legislation from the United States Congress is required in what is fundamental. In what concerns the complementary measures necessary to impose that legis- fully confirmed by virtue of all the laws of the congress of the United States in matters of rehabilitation imposed on Puerto Rico.
Let us be more explicit, because the crime of sowing false hopes in a people too suffered and deceived by the men who by duty should illustrate it is being committed.
The Costigan-Jones law on sugar restriction, like all North American rehabilitation laws, is implemented to help the rehabilitation of the United States. The American Congress and Mr. Roosevelt have exclusively in mind the interests of their Homeland and if for that purpose they need to suck our last drop of blood, they will do so, if we do not hasten to defend ourselves immediately against that exploitation in crescendo.
Said law fixes the sugar quota of the sugar producers of the United States, a quota that the president cannot alter; and that in case sugar consumption increases, the domestic Yankee producers will be the only ones benefited; nor are they imposed any restriction on the elaboration of collateral products such as alcohol.
And it is that the President, facing his North American fellow citizens, is subject to what the law provides.
Let us see the contrast in regard to Puerto Rico.
Our nation remains at the mercy of the very personal will of Mr. Roosevelt, as is required in every military regime. It is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the sugar quota; it is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the quotas for each sugar central; it is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the amount of refined sugar that will be exported to the United States.
The collateral products of the sugar industry such as alcohol may be elaborated exclusively for consumption in Puerto Rico. The North American market is forbidden.
