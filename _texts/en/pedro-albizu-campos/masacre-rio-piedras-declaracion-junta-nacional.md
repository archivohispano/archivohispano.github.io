In the edition of La Democracia dated October 28,
1935, and which circulated the night before, Colonel Riggs, chief
of the police in the service of the Yankee interventionist government, threatens the Puerto Rican nation
with a declaration of war. The Colonel says that there will be war, war and war!
It is necessary to note that the newspaper La Democracia is the
official organ of the Liberal Party, the governing party and currently
a fanatical defender of North American imperialism and of the administration of the President of the United States, Mr. Roosevelt.
No other newspaper has reproduced that interesting trilogy
of Colonel Riggs: war, war and war!
An act of war was the invasion of the United States Army
on July 25, 1898; an act of war constitutes the military intervention
that Puerto Rico has suffered since that fateful date; acts of war
are all the acts perpetrated by the Yankee interventionist power in
Puerto Rico during the thirty-six years of North American intervention.
The war has been waged in the name of Freedom,
of Equality, of Fraternity, of the Holy Bible, and paradoxically
also in the name of order and peace!
The nation has received the blows of war with the passive resistance
of its superior culture and its Christian civilization that have
prevented its annexation to the invading empire.
The intervening power has resorted to extreme measures of
war.
It has used all the weapons prohibited by civilization
to wage war between civilized peoples.
The goal of that war for the enemy has been none other than the
total extermination of all Puerto Ricans.

In January of 1932, as our contribution in defense of nations
unwary or confident in the good faith of the United States of
North America, we addressed the following circular to His Holiness The Pope, to the League of
Nations and to all civilized nations:

NATIONALIST PARTY OF PUERTO RICO
NATIONAL BOARD
Secretariat of Foreign Relations
Apartado 420

Sir:
In compliance with the instructions given by the President of the Nationalist Party of Puerto Rico, Don Pedro Albizu
Campos, the undersigned has the honor to inform
you of the serious matter that motivates this communication.
Under the guise of helping our people to solve public health problems, some time ago a North American medical corps of the Rockefeller Institute, an American institution that
works in close cooperation with the government of its country, the United States of North America, was established at the Presbyterian Hospital of San Juan of Puerto Rico. Apparently dedicated to curing patients victims of anemia and malaria, in reality, it has been developing a plan to exterminate our people by inoculating patients who have had the misfortune of falling under its direction, the virus of incurable diseases such as cancer.
At the date of the North American invasion, thirty-three years ago, we constituted a strong and healthy community. Wealth was well divided. There was work and abundance. The country produced almost everything it needed. There was practically no public debt. The general government and the city councils had substantial reserves in cash to face any crisis. We were a rich country in fact and in name, and joy was the common heritage of all Puerto Ricans and of the foreigners who lived with us. These facts were stated in the official reports of the first heads of the North American military occupation.
The invading power forced the displacement of Puerto Ricans
by its own North American nationals. More than fifty
thousand native landowners have disappeared and currently four
American companies own more than sixty percent of the
total wealth of the country. Barely
twenty percent of the national patrimony remains in the hands of the nationals.
The system implemented is simple: all the public burden falls
on the nationals; the Americans and their companies are practically exempt from all public contribution and enjoy the monopoly of franchises. The United States imposed its tariffs on Puerto
Rico and practically destroyed our foreign trade with other nations. This is its sixth market in the world, which averages approximately two hundred million dollars.
By virtue of this commercial monopoly, the Americans impose the conditions of purchase of our products and the conditions of sale of their products.
The mercantile monopoly is seconded by the financial monopoly.
There is practically no credit for Puerto Ricans.
The United States has mortgaged the country to its own financial interests. The military intervention destroyed agriculture.
It turned the country into a huge sugar plantation forcing it
to buy in the most expensive market in the world, that is, the North American market, which imposes on us merchandise not acceptable in any other part, under conditions and prices authorized by the monopoly it enjoys in our country. We depend on the food that the Americans sell us.
They launch on our market many products that are not fit for human consumption and that are the source of serious
diseases. There is no health regulation prohibiting the importation of food of that quality and epidemics increase.
The last head of the military occupation has just informed his government that more than sixty percent of the population is in chronic unemployment, without any means of support; that the average of salaries in the main industry, that is, the sugar industry, is one hundred and eighty dollars a year.
This means that sixty percent of the population is condemned to perish from hunger.

It did not escape the sagacity of the Yankee government in Puerto Rico that our supreme leadership had knowledge of its criminal purposes and its officials in Puerto Rico knew that it was common knowledge within the Nationalist Party of said plan due to the meticulous investigation carried out by this National Junta and communicated to all its affiliated organisms in the nation and abroad.
We were waiting to communicate to those Puerto Ricans who did not serve in the ranks of Nationalism to acquire greater documentation from the hands of the Yankee officials themselves in Puerto Rico.
The crimes perpetrated in Río Piedras on October 24, 1935 have precipitated events.
What was the pretext that the Yankee police, called insular, obtained to assassinate in their own closed car Don Ramón S. Pagán and his companions Don Eduardo Rodríguez Vega and Don Pedro Quiñones?
For greater clarity we reproduce the report issued by the Adjutant General of the police:
"Río Piedras, Oct. 25, At 11:00 A.M. yesterday, while the police were conducting under arrest Ramón S. Pagán, driver of automobile number 6268 and nationalist leader, because he had refused to give his personal information to the police, he fired shots at Lance Corporal Jorge Pérez wounding him in the right arm, originating a shootout between civilians occupying said automobile and the police, from which resulted dead Ramón S. Pagán, Pedro Quiñones from those in the car and wounded Dionisio Pearson and Eduardo Rodríguez. Juan Muñoz Jiménez, a person who was not intervening in the shootout, also resulted dead from a bullet. When the dead and the wounded were being taken to the Municipal Hospital, two bombs were thrown against the Insular Government automobile driven by policeman Jacobo Cebollero, destroying the left part. At those moments, another nationalist leader, José Santiago, sustained a shootout with policeman Angel Samuel Bonilla, using a regulation pistol, caliber 45. Santiago died. Another passerby Félix Cruz, received a bullet during the shootout. Arrested for carrying arms were Juan Sánchez Padín, Silvano Rivera González and José Maldonado Ruiz, who turned out to be nationalist leaders as well. Two bombs without firing, that is, loaded to make them explode, were also seized. Police reinforcements were sent from San Juan. The prosecutors Romaní and Quiñonez and the local Municipal Judge proceeded to the investigation of the facts. The authorities of the University of Puerto Rico suspended classes until further notice. Special police surveillance was established at the University and in the town of Río Piedras.
Eduardo Rodríguez, one of the civilians wounded yesterday in Río Piedras, died at six in the afternoon at the Municipal Hospital.
By order of the Chief of Police,

J. JUDICE
Auxiliary Chief and Adjutant".

That report says that Don Ramón S. Pagán was arrested "for having refused to give his personal information."
The Chief of Police of Río Piedras affirms that he had given orders to arrest Don Ramón S. Pagán under any pretext. Here is the account that said chief gave to the newspaper El Mundo:
"Beniamino discovers the automobile they described to him"
Chief Beniamino continued alone driving his automobile when upon passing through North Street he saw stopped to his right the automobile that the two detectives had described to him and in which they told him an individual he was pursuing had gotten into. It was a small Willys-77 automobile, sedan type and with plates 6268. Inside were four individuals, two in the front seat and two in the back seat.
Beniamino tells us that he continued on his way discreetly, as if he had not seen anything but he headed towards the place where he had left Corporal Pérez and guard Colón, whom he ordered to get into his automobile.

Stop that automobile

The Chief told the policemen that he had seen the automobile that the detectives had described to him on North Street and that they should stop the vehicle under any pretext."
(El Mundo, October 25, 1935, page 4).

There was a need "to stop the vehicle under any pretext" and a need to kill its occupants under any pretext.
The adjutant of the chief of the insular police and the chief of that corps in Río Piedras affirm that Don Ramón S. Pagán initiated the shootout by firing and wounding the police corporal who had gotten on with other policemen on the running boards of the automobile.
Eyewitnesses declare the contrary. The police riddled Don Ramón S. Pagán and his friends with bullets at point-blank range. He and they were shot at close range. Their faces appeared burned by the powder incrustated with projectiles fired at extremely close distance. The automobile presents proof that the shots were made from the side at point-blank range. The discharges that the police platoon made against Don Ramón S. Pagán and his companions were almost all made from behind the automobile. The vehicle has no less than eighteen perforations made by projectiles with entrance orifice in the rear part.
The automobile does not present a single shot received from the front.
The policemen positioned on the sides and in the rear of the automobile fired continuously against its defenseless occupants shouting: "Don't let them get out alive! Don't let them get out alive!"
We will reproduce the version of these facts as it appears in the English section of El Imparcial, edition of Friday, October 25, 1935. This section is in charge of North American journalist Mr. Holliday, from whom no prejudice against the government of his homeland, the United States of North America, can be expected:
"When Chief Beniamino arrived, however, he ordered Corporal Pérez, of the Insular Police, to stop the car. As the car approached, coming slowly down the street, Pérez leaped in the front of it, halled it and demanded to see the driver's license. Pagán who was driving, was unable to produce one. Pérez jumped on the running board ordered Pagán to drive to the police station."
Pérez fire brings on gun battle:
"As the car proceeded down Braumbaugh Street, between Arzuaga and De Diego streets, Pérez thought that Pagán was acting suspicious. Nervous as a cat, faced with five men who he was certain were armed, Pérez apparently lost his head, shot Pagán through the breast. Pagán drawing his own automatic 45, shot Pérez through the abdomen. Another shot from Pérez wounded Eduardo Rodríguez."

Police Gather:

"Pérez fell to the paving unconscious. Pagán apparently died within few seconds. The three unwounded occupants of the car removed Rodríguez who was seriously wounded, left him lying in the street while they attempted to get the car moving again. The motor had stalled when Pagán slumped over the wheel, Pérez bullet through his breast."

"Hearing the shots, Chief of Police Beniamino called other policeman to his assistance, hastened to the scene. The Nationalist were just getting the car started again when Beniamino and his aides closed in, aided by other police attracted to scene by the sound of the shots."

Nationalist put up fight
Cornered, the remaining men whipped out their weapons, began firing at the police, who took refuge behind tree trunks and the police car poured a veritable stream of bullets in the direction of the cornered Nationalists."

Once the assassination of Don Ramón S. Pagán and his companions was consummated, a pretext had to be invented to justify this vandalism of the police in the service of the Yankees. It has been wished to give to understand that it was necessary to perpetrate that assassination because it was feared that Don Ramón S. Pagán and his friends were going to disturb a certain student assembly that was to be held in the assembly hall of the University on the same day.

To the confession of the police chiefs, that body lacks instruction and knowledge to prevent the commission of a crime.

It has only one resource at its disposal in that case: to assassinate the presumed author before he can commit the supposed crime. If he is accompanied, assassinate his companions as well. If there are innocent people in the surroundings, assassinate them as well. That public peace is disturbed, bah! what interests the police is to assassinate a presumed author of a hypothetical crime.

That criterion it receives from its Yankee chief. The juridical apology of that criterion is made by none other than the attorney general of the Yankee regime in Puerto Rico.

That is a monstrosity, but it is the barbarous and bloodthirsty reality that seeks to impose itself on the Puerto Rican nation to sow terror, and with terror, absolute submission to the pretensions of the Yankee empire.

That monstrosity will not be tolerated. The Nation will rise in arms to reject it and dissolve it.

But, let us see the facts. At the moment the Yankee police detained Don Ramón S. Pagán and his friends, no student assembly was being held at the University. When he and his companions were assassinated, they were more than a kilometer from the grounds of the University, walking in the opposite direction from where the University is located. Their car was heading south on Braumbaugh Street. Every second it was moving further from the University. On that route came the arrest under any pretext, as the chief of police said, and Don Ramón S. Pagán was ordered to drive to the police station, which was even further south. The police confess that he complied with that order. Furthermore, he allowed policemen to get on both running boards of the vehicle, despite this being a small five-passenger sedan. Behind came a police escort in a Yankee government car armed with carbines.

In the first place it is contrary to the laws of the Yankee regime itself to take anyone arrested to a police station. When a person commits a crime it is the duty of the police to file the corresponding complaint and take the accused before a judge or prosecutor to give the accused the opportunity to post bail. If the accused cannot post bail he will be taken to jail, but never to a police station.

When one intends to take an accused to a police station, all that is intended is to torture the accused to extract confessions from him. If he dies in the torture, there will be no witnesses to the assassination. The prosecutors of the regime will take care of exonerating the police if they receive the corresponding superior Yankee order.

Nevertheless, Don Ramón S. Pagán submitted to being taken to the station. As he offered no opportunity to the police to start a fight, they riddled him and his companions with bullets without further delay. A superior order had to be fulfilled. Everything had been prepared by the corresponding officials of the Yankee regime.

Whatever the pretext for arresting Don Ramón S. Pagán, it was impossible to invent one for arresting his friends. It was clear that the Yankee regime preferred that there be no survivor to tell what had happened. The evidence had to be suppressed and they were murdered in cold blood. They had the right to get out of the automobile, but the police shouted, "Don't let them get out alive! Don't let them get out alive!"

This monstrous crime was perpetrated in the most central location of the university city, near the church.
The police wounded a large number of passersby and killed Don Juan Muñoz Jiménez. This is confessed by policeman Alejandro Castillo, badge number 16. In La Democracia, edition of October twenty-fifth, 1935, page eight, the following information appears:
"What Policeman Alejandro Castillo tells us"
"Insular policeman Alejandro Castillo, badge 16, was an eyewitness to the events. He tells us that he was near the Police Station when he heard the detonations. He ran to the site of the events and found Corporal Jorge Pérez wounded under an automobile. He picked him up and headed with him toward the Municipal Hospital. Policeman Castillo informs us that when the bullets of the instigators of the tragedy were directed at him, the police in order to prevent him from being hit by the projectiles fired against their opponents, with the misfortune that their bullets fell on the body of citizen Don Juan Muñoz Jiménez, who was getting off a White Star Line bus, the victim dying on the spot. In that way the life of policeman Castillo was saved, while that of Mr. Muñoz was lost."
A few minutes later the children were leaving school. It gives one horror to think of the carnage these savage policemen would have made.

As it has been insinuated that Don Ramón S. Pagán and his valiant companions might possibly have been going to interrupt an assembly that the students of the University were to hold, we must clarify the particulars of that crude pretext for an assassination.
At the moment when Don Ramón S. Pagán and his companions were arrested they were more than a kilometer from the University walking south, that is, moving away from said center of instruction. To return to the University they needed to cross the Río Piedras plaza and the entire center of the city heading north.
When they were arrested the assembly hall of the University was closed. Of course, no assembly was being held at that moment.
As soon as it became known at the University that Don Ramón S. Pagán and his companions had been assassinated in the Río Piedras plaza, the assembly hall of the University was immediately opened and the student body was convened for the assembly in question.
It is logical to think that the beginning of the assembly had to be synchronized with an act of terrorism perpetrated by the police in the Yankee service. The clear purpose of the intervening power was to intimidate the nationalist students of the University and the student body in general so that they would leave the assembly to the free will of the four or five students in the service of the Yankee government who were to approve a resolution project declaring our President "Enemy Number 1." All that policy of violence and despotism has done is to accentuate the nationalist spirit within the University and has increased the fervor of the student body for the cause of the independence of Puerto Rico.

Ten days later, by radio from Maunabo, our President repeated those same concepts for all the youth of Puerto Rico.

The immense crowd gathered on Sunday, October 20 in Maunabo, on the occasion of the radio broadcast, gave our President an ovation upon finishing to encourage him in his patriotic work.

As proof of the unanimous reception of his speech, it suffices to point out that the three presidents of the three political parties represented in Maunabo, that is, Liberal, Unión Republicana and Socialista, accompanied by members of their respective political committees, presented themselves at the home where our President was lodging to congratulate him for his speech and offer him their patriotic solidarity.

That congratulation was one of the many that our President received from all parts of the nation for the same reason.
