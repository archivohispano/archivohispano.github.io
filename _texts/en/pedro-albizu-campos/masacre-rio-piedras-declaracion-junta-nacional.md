Upon a promontory of humid earth, they bid farewell in mourning,
with eloquent speeches delivered by Mr. Ermelindo Santiago,
who spoke on behalf of the Homeland Lodge, to which
Mr. Ramón S. Pagán belonged; Carrera, as president of the National
Student Federation, and the President of the Nationalist Party,
Licenciado Pedro Albizu Campos.
Mr. Albizu Campos said, among other things, that there
"entire Puerto Rico was represented, from Cabo Rojo
to Vieques; from west to east and from north to south, all sharing
the immense mourning for freedom." He then said that adhesions
to the mourning had already been received from various nations; that "the Yankee enemy wants the blood of Puerto Ricans to flow"; that "today they
will use one faction to carry out their purposes and tomorrow they will use
another." "From the tomb of freedom, I ask that Benigno Fernández García, the Attorney General, bring us the
accusations against those police officers who murdered Pagán and his companions,
and murdered José Santiago, when he had already surrendered,
when he was unarmed." He said that Colonel Riggs sent the
uniformed forces to Río Piedras "with the deliberate purpose of
murdering the nationalist representation of Puerto Rico." He similarly accused
Governor Winship, and Chancellor Chardón of
"taking it out on the student body."

The Massacre of
Río Piedras: Declaration
of the National Board
In the edition of La Democracia dated October 28,
1935, which circulated the night before, Colonel Riggs, chief
of the police in the service of the Yankee interventionist government, threatens the Puerto Rican nation
with a declaration of war. The Colonel says that there will be war, war, and war!
It is necessary to note that the newspaper La Democracia is the
official organ of the Liberal Party, the governing party and currently
a fanatical defender of North American imperialism and of the administration of the President of the United States, Mr. Roosevelt.
No other newspaper has reproduced that interesting trilogy
of Colonel Riggs: war, war, and war!
An act of war was the invasion of the United States army
on July 25, 1898; an act of war is the military intervention
that Puerto Rico has suffered since that nefarious date; acts of war
are all the acts perpetrated by the Yankee interventionist power in
Puerto Rico during the thirty-six years of North American intervention.
The war has been waged in the name of Liberty, of
Equality, of Fraternity, of the Holy Bible, and paradoxically
also in the name of order and peace!
The nation has received the blows of war with the passive resistance of its superior culture and its Christian civilization, which have prevented its annexation to the invading empire.
The interventionist power has resorted to extreme measures of war.
It has used all the weapons prohibited by civilization
to wage war between civilized peoples.
The goal of that war for the enemy has been none other than the
total extermination of all Puerto Ricans.
La Palabra, November 4, 1935, p. 2, 4, 5, and 6.

In compliance with the instructions imparted by the President of the Nationalist Party of Puerto Rico, Don Pedro Albizu
Campos, the undersigned has the honor to inform
you of the grave matter that motivates this communication.
Under the guise of helping our people solve public health problems, some time ago a North American medical corps from the Rockefeller Institute was established at the Presbyterian Hospital of San Juan de Puerto Rico, an American institution that works in close cooperation with the government of its country, the United States of North America. Apparently dedicated to curing patients who are victims of anemia and malaria, in reality, it has been developing a plan to exterminate our people by inoculating patients who have had the misfortune of falling under its direction with the virus of incurable diseases such as cancer.
On the date of the North American invasion, thirty-three years ago, we constituted a strong and healthy community. Wealth
was well divided. There was work and abundance. The country produced
almost everything it needed. There was practically no public debt.
The general government and the municipalities had substantial
reserves in cash to face any crisis. We were a
country rich in fact and in name, and joy was the common heritage of all Puerto Ricans and of the foreigners who lived with us. These facts were stated in the official reports of the first chiefs of the North American military occupation.

The invading power forced the displacement of Puerto Ricans by its own North American nationals. More than fifty thousand native landowners have disappeared and currently four American companies own more than sixty percent of the total wealth of the country. The natives scarcely retain
twenty percent of the national patrimony.
The system implemented is simple: the entire public burden falls
on the natives; the North Americans and their companies are practically exempt from all public contribution and enjoy the monopoly of franchises. The United States imposed its tariffs on Puerto Rico and practically destroyed our foreign commerce with other nations. This is its sixth market in the world,
which averages approximately two hundred million dollars.
By virtue of this commercial monopoly, the North Americans impose the conditions of purchase of our products and the conditions of sale of their products.
The mercantile monopoly is seconded by the financial monopoly. There is practically no credit for Puerto Ricans. Any wealth in their hands or to destroy it if none of them wanted to acquire it.
The United States has mortgaged the country to its own financial interests. The military intervention destroyed agriculture.
It turned the country into an enormous sugar plantation, forcing it
to buy in the most expensive market in the world, that is, the North American market, which imposes on us merchandise not acceptable
anywhere else, under conditions and prices authorized by the monopoly it enjoys in our country. We depend on the food that the North Americans sell us.
They launch on our market many products that are not fit for human consumption and which are the source of serious
diseases. There is no health regulation that prohibits the importation of food of that quality and epidemics increase.
The last chief of the military occupation has just informed his
government that more than sixty percent of the population is in
chronic unemployment, without any means of livelihood; that the average of
salaries in the main industry, that is, the sugar industry, is one hundred and eighty dollars a year.
This means that sixty percent of the population is
condemned to perish from hunger. Deaths have already been recorded.
