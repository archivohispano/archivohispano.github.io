Manifesto before the visit
of the president of
the United States

The nation awaits another unwelcome visit. Once again a leader of the North American empire seeks to cross the threshold of the Homeland without prior invitation from anyone in Puerto Rico. Aboard a warship, with a Squadron as escort, he forces entry into our ports. He comes as commander-in-chief of all the sea and land forces of the United States of North America to inspect his subordinates in charge of maintaining the North American military intervention in our country.

Under this regime of force, the personal will of the head of the empire is the government, in accordance with the Constitution and the North American laws. The subordinate bodies, such as the insular legislature, the courts, and administrative entities, must function according to his desire, or he can ignore them or suppress them with a simple decree of martial law, without having to wait for the prior consent of any authority. Responsible in person for the complete administration, he must be invested with all the powers.

To attend to the internal conveniences of his cabinet, Mr. Roosevelt resolved to transfer, by himself, by virtue of a decree, the affairs of Puerto Rico from the Department of War to the Department of the Interior, of the North American Government. Of course, neither the insular legislature nor the political parties most loyal to the regime were consulted. Neither the Yankee Government nor its current head has given reasons for this change. Foreign despotism, in order to be respectable, will impose itself without explanations.

El Mundo, June 30, 1934, p.2 and 8.

Some who are obliged to be apologists for such conduct have tried to justify the unjustifiable.

The nation will remember that in the United States there is no ministry or ministers with their own constitutional responsibility. The President personally is the executive power, and the people who are part of his cabinet are his private secretaries; as such, he has the power, the right to transfer, for his own convenience, any matter that by law falls under his jurisdiction, such as the administration of a nation under the military intervention of his country. Thus, the North American Presidency had the affairs of Puerto Rico entrusted to its Secretary of State, then transferred them to its Secretary of War; and now, it places them under the aegis of its Secretary of the Interior. All of which can be done because he is constitutional head of the Army, the Navy and all the armed institutes of his Government, and under his direction necessarily falls all military intervention in nations coveted by the Yankee empire, as happens in the case of Puerto Rico.

The mysterious rehabilitation plan for Puerto Rico fully illustrates that situation. Nobody knows anything about the plan. The naive believed that an insular legislature existed here, until recently a Yankee customs court declared null a law approved by that body, establishing precepts for the protection of our coffee in our domestic market. That Yankee court says bluntly that the insular legislature has no powers to deal with something serious like that.

Mr. Roosevelt ignores the existence, as is customary, of all the subordinate entities of the regime under his command, insular legislature, and administrative entities, and by himself, appoints a commission to draw up a complete rehabilitation plan for Puerto Rico that he himself will be in charge of implementing.

Nobody knows anything about this plan, we repeat. No one has been consulted. Mr. Roosevelt will announce it personally when he arrives in Puerto Rico to impose it on the country immediately.

Tyranny is exercised without diplomacy. Those most loyal to the regime who have some shame keep a significant silence.

The salaried pretend to be enthusiastic about the visit of the head of the North American empire and want to raise the people's hands to applaud Mr. Roosevelt.

The nation is standing and serenely contemplates those who, protected by force, have the audacity to proclaim themselves arbiters of its destinies. Mr. Roosevelt has been administering Puerto Rico for fifteen months. As was to be expected, the only thing he has done is to continue the policy outlined by his predecessors to demolish the economy of Puerto Rico, and thus turn a great nation of free and happy citizens into a herd of pawns, into a sugar batey at the mercy of Yankee overseers. Mr. Roosevelt has increased the consumption taxes paid by the people, in the respectable sum that amounts to more than twenty-five million dollars annually, to enrich the industrialist and the farmer of his country.

Within the Yankee tariff fence, Puerto Rico has lost all its favorable trade balance, which has gone to the Yankee coffers, a sum that amounts to some four hundred million dollars in thirty-four (34) years. Within the same regime of Yankee commercial monopoly, forced to sell at the price that the enemy imposes and to buy from him at the price that he dictates to us, conservative calculations show a loss for Puerto Rico for the same period of time of some six hundred million dollars.

The Administration of Mr. Roosevelt has not believed sufficient that tribute of one billion dollars during the presence of the Yankee military forces in Puerto Rico, and raises the tribute at a rate of some twenty-five million additional per year.

The United States feels its international isolation, the loss of its markets and the concerns of a social crisis. The Yankee financial oligarchy had lost its popular prestige, and Mr. Roosevelt has restored it in a short time. The working masses, with twelve million men without work and without opportunity to organize themselves before the absolute power of capital, are in worse conditions than before he came to power, due to the rise in the products of prime necessity without the corresponding rise in salaries. The strikes are resolved in the traditional Yankee way, machine-gunning the people; and before feeding the indigent, the food is destroyed so that it will rise in price, and the consumer will pay the capitalist the cost of the reduction. The ignorant Yankee masses are anesthetized with the mellifluous verb of the present occupant of Casa Blanca.
