SELECTED WORKS
It suffices what has been observed to see how the Yankee intervention has destroyed Puerto Rican liquid capital, placing the nation at the mercy of North American banking usury.

The regime has one virtue, which is frankly exploitative, and thus we see how the same bank that in New York lends at four percent, in Puerto Rico does so at nine. To legitimize this usury, the loan at twelve percent annually is maintained as law.

It is clear that cheap money cannot be obtained for any Puerto Rican enterprise. Industry, to exist, must be based on miserable wages for the worker.

The government of the United States of North America sent a scientific commission to inventory our national wealth and specify what should be reserved for its nationals and what had to be destroyed when left in Puerto Rican hands. The selection was determined by the demand of the North American market. This was limited to the consumption of tobacco and sugar. The intervening power then resolved to promote the exclusive production of tobacco and sugar, raised the corporations for that purpose, and immediately began the demolition of all other wealth, coffee, food products, fruit trees, forests, etc., stimulating the spontaneous destruction of agriculture, the centuries-old national wealth, through its propaganda methods, which would prompt landowners to plant tobacco in the highlands and sugar in the lowlands, crops that require the total destruction of any other planting. The Yankee corporation became the only bank of a farmer reduced to complete slavery, because he had been deprived even of the most elementary subsistence, if the corresponding credit was denied him.

The refaccionaria corporation would also fix the price of tobacco and sugar and thus ruined its colonists, who became foremen or day laborers on the farms where they were owners, or professional politicians addicted to the same intervening regime that had ruined them.

To accelerate the process of supplantation, the power exonerated its interests from contributions and made the native landowner and the worker bear the entire fiscal weight with the consumption tax.

It stimulated loans and the public burden to such an extent that currently the dead fiscal weight on the nation is thirty million dollars annually, as the totality of insular and municipal expenses and amortization of the public debt, of which the worker pays twenty million, vellón a vellón in consumption contributions.

The nation lacking income, to face such a burden, has been liquidating its capitals by the forced route.

Yankee latifundismo has cornered. Sixty-four thousand landowners, who represent no less than half a million with their families, have entered the ranks of the proletariat.

In this competition of workers, women have entered, out of terrible necessity, and national production systematically reduced to tobacco and sugar, the natural competition to find work has been as fierce as the struggle for public positions, leading the salary to be the lowest in the world.

The refaccionaria corporation gave encouragement to its colonists to buy the small plots where the classes of economic modesty lived, facilitating them money for the maneuver, but the colonist did not see that to acquire the money, he mortgaged all his assets with a sum that would be impossible for him to pay, because the enemy regulated the price of tobacco and sugar at will.

The native was stimulated to believe himself a latifundista as well, to, once fattened, cut off his head.

The result has been that more than half of the sugar values are Yankee in full dominion, or the rest is mortgaged to Yankee banks as well. It is true that there are some Puerto Ricans around who call themselves sugar producers, and who, for greater irony, act as spokesmen for sugar slavery. The Yankees, who suffer from stupid arrogance, do not confuse themselves with appearances, and in the association of sugar producers they appear occupying a secondary role.

God grant that independence can be proclaimed before the Puerto Rican mandatories of the sugar "trust" lose everything.

Rural villages founded in healthy sites and fertile lands have disappeared; the sugar latifundio has swept them away like a fearsome hurricane, throwing the population onto urban areas, increasing competition in labor and with it unemployment, to such an extent that we have the highest number of forced unemployed in the world, that is, seventy percent.

SELECTED WORKS

With agriculture ruined, we are at the mercy of the Yankee market.

Puerto Rico is a besieged plaza, and its inhabitants are supplied with what their enemies want, at a price and quality of their exclusive convenience.

There is a Department of Agriculture, but it does not promote any agriculture.

To justify what it costs the taxpayer to sustain it so that it can carry out the investigations that the sugar producers order it to, it is entertained by preparing the children of those who have no land to cultivate to be good day laborers, in the special schools that are called Rural Units.

The sugar corporation has closed all the ports of the nation with the exception of San Juan and Mayagüez.

Mayagüez remains open thanks to the exploitative needle industry.

Ponce, the first port of Puerto Rico before the invasion, is today a pile of ruins.

With the closure of the ports, the cabotage service has ended, and all maritime activity of importance.

The nation has neither entry nor exit.

The sugar corporation ruined commerce. It has its warehouses distributed scientifically in its colonies. It sells everything. It imports it in its own ships. It does not pay any of the contributions that commerce supports.

It does not pay in money to force the day laborer to buy in its stores. In this way it collects the money it pays in wages, doing a fabulous business. At the end of the week, the day laborer has, at most, a peseta left over, which is the only thing he can spend in a nearby establishment. He remains immobilized in the batey because he does not have the means to make even the shortest trip.

The batey is a generally unhealthy place. The agregado has to be a perfect slave, docile, and obedient to the patronal will or they put him on the road, with his wife and his children at any moment. Since the laws do not govern for the sugar producers, not even the provisions on the eviction law are respected, the law is violence. The entire government, from the Governor down, is at the disposal of the sugar will. There is no guarantee whatsoever for the worker if he lives in the batey. He has almost seven months of invernazo without any income.

The workers in this industry, the hardest that can be imagined, earned an average of eighty-five cents a day in the past harvest.

The government signed an agreement with the sugar producers reducing wages. This has been confessed by the Central Aguirre itself in a loose sheet.

The government and the sugar producers constitute a single person. The government has agreed with itself and wants to impose on the workers the conditions that suit the sugar industry.

It has mobilized the Police to intimidate the strikers. In Guayama there are more than two hundred policemen. The carts and trains of cane pass guarded by the public force, because the strike is much feared.

The government is a strikebreaker. The strike that was defunct by the will of the officious ex-leaders of obrerismo, today revived, has had immediate success because to encourage the strikebreakers they pay them twenty percent more than what is provided in the official agreement.

Nevertheless, the strike is standing in the south and in the east under the direction of the Asociación de Trabajadores de Puerto Rico, which is adding to its bosom all the workers of the nation in a saving movement for the proletarian and for the Homeland.

What do the strikers ask for? Something very modest. That no one work twelve hours a day anywhere; that it be taken into account that the cost of living has increased, doubling the price of many essential products, but the worker with inexplicable resignation is limited to demanding a forty percent increase; that he be paid in money; that the law be fulfilled.

When it is said that a worker earns eighty cents, in reality a salary of forty-five cents is set, taking into account the drop in the purchasing power of the dollar.

The government wants to impose these enslaving conditions on the workers who represent ninety percent of the population. This is the sugar slavery imposed by force. It is up to us to abolish it.

San Juan,
January 18, 1934.

Albizu Campos, speaker
powerful and brilliant, delivers a speech to the workers of the cane during the strike of the sugar industry in January 1934.
