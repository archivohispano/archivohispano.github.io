The Nation is standing and serenely contemplates those who, sheltered by force, have the audacity to proclaim themselves arbiters of its destinies. Mr. Roosevelt has been administering Puerto Rico for fifteen months. As was to be expected, all he has done is continue the policy outlined by his predecessors to demolish the economy of Puerto Rico, and thus convert a great nation of free and happy citizens into a herd of peons, into a sugar batey at the mercy of Yankee overseers. Mr. Roosevelt has increased consumer taxes paid by the people, in the respectable sum that amounts to more than twenty-five million dollars annually, to enrich the industrialist and the farmer of his country.

Within the Yankee tariff fence, Puerto Rico has lost all its favorable trade balance, which has gone to the Yankee coffers, a sum that amounts to some four hundred million dollars in thirty-four (34) years. Within the same regime of Yankee commercial monopoly, forced to sell at the price that the enemy imposes and to buy from them at the price they dictate to us, conservative calculations yield a loss for Puerto Rico for the same period of time of some six hundred million dollars.

The Administration of Mr. Roosevelt has not considered sufficient that tribute of one billion dollars during the presence of the Yankee military forces in Puerto Rico, and raises the tribute at a rate of some twenty-five million additional per year.

The United States feels its international isolation, the loss of its markets and the worries of a social crisis. The Yankee financial oligarchy had lost its popular prestige, and Mr. Roosevelt has restored it in a short time. The working masses, with twelve million men without work and without opportunity to organize before the absolute power of capital, are in worse conditions than before he came to power, due to the rise in the prices of essential goods without the corresponding rise in wages. Strikes are resolved in the traditional Yankee way, machine-gunning the people; and before feeding the indigent, food is destroyed so that it rises in price, and the consumer will pay the capitalist the cost of the reduction. The ignorant Yankee masses are anesthetized with the mellifluous verb of the current occupant of the White House.

In foreign relations, there have not been those triumphs, despite the declarations of "good neighborliness". Yankee imperialism wants to consolidate what it has and has simulated concessions in Cuba, Nicaragua and Haiti. Its ambition is always the order of the day and its Press reveals its true intentions in Colombia.

Mr. Roosevelt is coming to Puerto Rico on his way to Haiti, Colombia and Panama. Here he will say some kind words for our America, its culture and its civilization. He, like every enlightened North American, knows that Iberian America is one at its core, and that Puerto Rico is part of that great whole that is the object of North American greed.

In such circumstances, the announced visit of the President of the United States of North America is doubly unwelcome.

Since the rehabilitation of the United States is a matter of many years, that same time will be needed to rehabilitate Puerto Rico, if it continues under North American rule.

Mr. Roosevelt did not commit himself to anything, and since the conversion into law someday, sooner or later, of the suggestions and advice and opinions of Mr. Chardón, Menéndez Ramos and Fernández García depends on his very personal will, and since it is already known what the Yankee rehabilitating plan applied to Puerto Rico is, by virtue of the agricultural adjustment laws, the restriction of sugar production and the executive decrees complementary to those laws, it is puerile to take seriously any of the suggestions, opinions or advice of Mr. Chardón, Menéndez Ramos and Fernández García, because the laws already established and the complementary decrees of those laws are in function in all their vigor and their provisions are categorical; and nothing of what is current law, if the validity of the North American legislative provisions in Puerto Rico is admitted, authorizes any sensible person to waste their time even reading the brought and taken plan that has resulted in a Pandora's box by force.

Let us be more explicit, because the crime of sowing false hopes in a people too suffered and deceived by the men who by duty should illustrate it is being committed.

The Costigan-Jones sugar restriction law, like all North American rehabilitation laws, is implemented to help the rehabilitation of the United States. The American Congress and Mr. Roosevelt have in mind exclusively the interests of their Homeland and if for that purpose they need to suck our last drop of blood, they will do it, if we do not hurry to defend ourselves immediately against that exploitation in crescendo.

Said law fixes the sugar quota of the sugar producers of the United States, a quota that the president cannot alter; and that in case the sugar consumption increases, the domestic Yankee producers will be the only ones benefited; they are not imposed either any restriction on the elaboration of collateral products like alcohol.

And it is that the President, in front of his North American fellow citizens, is subject to what the law provides.

Let us see the contrast in regards to Puerto Rico.

Our Nation is at the mercy of the very personal will of Mr. Roosevelt, as is de rigueur in every military regime. It is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the sugar quota; it is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the quotas for each sugar central; it is Mr. Roosevelt who fixes the amount of refined sugar that will be exported to the United States. The collateral products of the sugar industry such as alcohol can be elaborated exclusively for consumption in Puerto Rico. The North American market remains closed.

Puerto Rican spokesmen of Mr. Roosevelt have not dared to comment on the quotas that he has fixed by virtue of executive decree and have managed to divert public indignation against those executive provisions of the Yankee President towards his Secretary of Agriculture Mr. Wallace, who has been wanted to be held responsible for said administrative provisions.
