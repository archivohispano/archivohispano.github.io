CHRONOLOGY OF PEDRO ALBIZU CAMPOS

By J. Benjamín Torres

1893: June 29. Born in the city of Ponce to parents
Juliana Campos y Campos and Alejandro Albizu y
Romero. He completed his primary education in the public schools
of Juana Diaz and Ponce.
1912: Travels to study at the University of Vermont in the United
States on a scholarship from the Aurora Lodge of Ponce.
1913: Moves to Harvard University to continue his
studies on the recommendation of two professors.
1916: Receives his bachelor's degree, pursues post-graduate studies and
enters the Harvard University Law School.
1921: Finishes his studies. Obtains diplomas in Chemical Engineering;
Letters and Philosophy, Military Sciences and Law.
Returns to Puerto Rico.
1922: Marries in Juana Diaz with Dr. Laura Meneses, of
Peruvian nationality and whom he had met at Harvard. On the 17th
of September 1922 the Nationalist Party is founded in Rio Piedras
of Puerto Rico, but Albizu Campos does not participate
in the organization of this group.
1923: Albizu Campos makes his formal entry into the Union Party of
Puerto Rico.
1924: March 26. His son Pedro Albizu Meneses is born in Ponce. In
the month of May he breaks with the Union Party and joins the
Nationalist Party. On May 18 he is elected first
vice-president of the Nationalist Party.
1925: August 30. His daughter Rosa is born in Ponce. On September 6
the Nationalist Party commissions him to visit the countries
Latin Americans in a propaganda campaign for the ideal
nationalist.
1927: June 20. Begins the pilgrimage through Latin America. Visits
firstly Santo Domingo, then Haiti, Cuba, Mexico,
Panama, Peru and Venezuela. On October 16 his
daughter Laura Esperanza.
1930: January 4. Returns to Puerto Rico. He goes to reside in the Villa
Palmeras neighborhood of Santurce. On May 11 he is elected president of the
Nationalist Party in the assembly that the group held
at the Ateneo and begins a gigantic campaign of education and
awareness of the people throughout all the fields, neighborhoods and
public squares of Puerto Rico. His motto: "Nationalism is
the Homeland organized to rescue its sovereignty."
1932: April 16. The Legislature of Puerta de Tierra presents a
project to convert the mono-starred flag of Puerto
Rico in standard of the colony. Albizu Campos, together with the
crowd that commemorated the birth of José De Diego in

8

San Juan takes the Capitol by assault to protest the
attempt by the colonized to desecrate the national symbol. To
penetrate the crowd to the Capitol, the handrails yielded and
several people fell and the young Manuel died
Rafael Suárez Diaz, the first martyr of the struggle of
Nationalism.
1934: January. Albizu Campos leads the strike of agricultural
workers. The sugar corporations are alarmed by the
intervention of Albizu Campos in the conflict and conspire
to paralyze his action through bribery or assassination.
1935: August. The Nationalist Party receives confidences from the
centers of North American power in Puerto Rico itself that
revealed the firm purpose of the North American regime to
have the leaders of the party assassinated under any pretext
Nationalist and its president, Albizu Campos. Don
Ramón S. Pagán was one of the men who corroborated the
existence of the plan that was directed by the secret police
North American in Puerto Rico. October 24: The police,
directed by Colonel Elisha Francis Riggs, murders in Río
Piedras to the nationalists Ramón S. Pagán, Pedro Quiñones,
Eduardo Rodríguez Vega and José Santiago Barea. Other
nationalist, Dionisio Pearson, was seriously injured. This
event is known as: The Río Piedras Massacre. 25 of
October: Albizu Campos delivers a farewell speech
of mourning and swears in those present so that this murder will not
go unpunished: "We come here to take an oath so that
this murder does not go unpunished. Raise your hand high
all those who believe themselves free. We all swear that the murder will not
endure in Puerto Rico."
1936: February 23: The young nationalists Hiram Rosado and Elías
Beauchamp execute in San Juan Colonel Elisha Francis
Riggs, Chief of Police, for being responsible for the Massacre
of Río Piedras, Rosado and Beauchamp were arrested,
locked up in the Police Headquarters in San Juan and
murdered there by the police themselves. February 24:
Albizu Campos again delivers the speech of mourning:
"We have already brought here precious ashes of other heroes. The
Nationalism has brought to the Homeland the transmutation of its being,
because man was not born to vegetate or to be fat and
strong; man was born to stand on supreme
principles in accordance with his immortality. To enter the
immortality there is only one entrance: the door of valor that
leads to sacrifice for a supreme cause. You have to
sacrifice yourself for the independence of the Homeland." March 4: The
United States District Court in Puerto Rico (Federal Court)
issued an arrest warrant against Albizu Campos and
other nationalist leaders under the accusation of "conspiring to
forcibly overthrow the government of the United States in Puerto
Rico". July 14: The trial against the leadership begins

nationalist. Are prosecuted: Pedro Albizu Campos, Juan
Antonio Corretjer, Erasmo Velázquez, Juan Gallardo Santiago,
Julio H. Velázquez, Pablo Rosado Ortiz, Clemente Soto Vélez
and Luis Florencio Velázquez. Acted as lawyers
defenders: Pedro Albizu Campos, Gilberto Concepción de
Gracia, Julio Pinto Gandía and J.M. Toro Nazario. The prosecutors
who intervened in the process were: A. Cecil Snyder,
Marcelino Romaní, Jorge Ortiz Toro, James E. Ruffin and Amos
W.W. Woodcock, The process was presided over by Judge Robert Cooper.
July 20: The jury composed of seven Puerto Ricans and
five North Americans failed to agree and was
dissolved by Judge Cooper. The five North Americans
voted for the conviction and the seven Puerto Ricans voted for
the absolution. July 27: the second trial against the
nationalist leadership. This time the jury was composed of
ten North Americans and two Puerto Ricans linked to the
North American interests. July 31: at 12:12 in the
morning the jury rendered a guilty verdict, were
sentenced from six to ten years in prison and transferred to the
La Princesa prison while an appeal was being filed.
1937: February 12: The United States Supreme Court
confirmed the sentence imposed on the nationalist leadership by the
Federal Court in San Juan. March 21: In Ponce the Board
Nationalist Municipal organized a parade of Cadets and a
meeting in the plaza of Ponce in protest of the imprisonment
of the nationalist leadership. The police opposed the activity and
mobilized hundreds of well-armed agents with
instructions to shoot to kill. When the nationalists
set out to start the parade on the corner of Marina and
Aurora streets, the police began the shooting massacring 19
people and leaving about 200 people badly injured. This
event is known as: The Ponce Massacre. June 7:
Albizu Campos and the other seven nationalist leaders are
transferred to the Penitentiary of Atlanta, Georgia, in the United
States.
1943: June: Albizu Campos leaves dying from the Atlanta Jail
and is confined in the Columbus Hospital in New York.
1945: After two years of treatment at Columbus Hospital
decides to return to Puerto Rico, but the authorities
North Americans prevent him from returning to his Homeland and he sees
forced to remain in the city of New York rendering
reports to the federal authorities of their addresses. He had the
city for jail.
1947: December 15: Albizu Campos returns to Puerto Rico after
extinguishing six years of imprisonment in the dungeons of Atlanta,
two of hospitalization and another two of probation in New
York. In the act of welcome that the people celebrate for him
Puerto Rican and the Nationalist Party in the Sixto Park

Escobar of San Juan, Albizu reaffirmed himself in his open struggle and
frank against despotism and colonialism. He pronounced his
famous words: "I have never been absent...". Begins the
total reorganization of the Nationalist Party and prepares to
give a revolutionary blow to the colonial regime.
1950: October 30: The Nationalist Insurrection breaks out. Albizu
Campos is arrested after a dramatic battle in front of his
home on the corner of Sol and Cruz Streets in San Juan, the
capital city.
1951: February: Albizu Campos is prosecuted and sentenced to 80
years in prison.
1953: September 30: Muñoz Marín pardons him due to pressure
exerted by the Latin American peoples in favor of the
excarceration of the nationalist leader. The pardon coincides with
a delicate situation of his health.
1954: March 1st: The nationalist heroes Lolita Lebrón Soto,
Rafael Cancel Miranda, Irvin Flores and Andrés Figueroa
Cordero attack the House of Representatives in the
United States Capitol. Muñoz Marín revokes the pardon to
Albizu Campos granted five months before and orders his
immediate arrest. After another dramatic gunfight in front
at his home in San Juan Albizu Campos was taken unconscious
and imprisoned again.
1956: April: Albizu Campos suffers in jail a thrombosis that
leaves him hemiparalytic and speechless. He is transferred to the Hospital
Presbyterian where a heavily guarded cell-room is enabled.
1964: November 15: Muñoz Marín aware that Albizu
Campos is on the verge of death, pardons him for the second time.
Muñoz Marín did not want Albizu Campos to die in the
jail.
1965: April 21: Albizu Campos, called "The Last Liberator of
America", dies in San Juan. His widow, Dr. Laura
Meneses declared: "The murder is consummated. Albizu
Campos died in prison. Albizu Campos has not died in
his house; when he arrived there he was already dead."
