Also, as part of the high policy to be followed, it must take into
account, very seriously, despite the invading force that may
support it, the opinion of the subjected nation, which cannot lose faith in
the administration of justice.

We understand that it is difficult for a Puerto Rican to occupy a
position in the judiciary if he meditates on the responsibility he assumes by
accepting the position.

A terrible dilemma assails him; how to unconditionally support the
regime and at the same time be loyal to his Homeland of origin.

Therefore, those compatriots who have not communed with the
North American absorption plan and who have wished to see their country free from
foreign interventions, have resigned from their judicial positions to
dedicate themselves to defending the cause of Independence; likewise, many
annexationists in the same situation have submitted their resignation before
launching themselves to preach statehood, thus respecting the opinion of the country, which
expects its citizens, that, given the difficulty of administering justice
in an irresponsible regime, they should not create obstacles for the exercise
of that ministry through the political propaganda of judges or officials
judicial during the exercise of their positions.

This principle has been imposed and no transgressor has escaped
public disapproval for his unconsidered action contrary to it.
In general, the judiciary that this imperial regime has imposed on us
has adhered to this principle of high ethics despite the grave
official pressure that has been exerted on it.

We all owe it this public recognition, despite the
absurd and unpatriotic actions of some judges and judicial officials.

We esteemed Mr. Emilio Del Toro as a man of sufficient
equanimity and character, who, despite his annexationist ideas, would
never be the person who would try to break with this severe norm.

But, to the general remonstrance for his unfortunate speech of July
Fourth, he has responded with a challenge full of arrogance to the Puerto Rican
nation.

Here are his words: "To expound my ideas, my judicial positions have not been and will not be
obstacles."

He fervently awaits "the complete freedom that we will enjoy on a
not distant day, when the star of Borinquen shines resplendently in the
American constellation as the symbol of one more state within the bosom of
the union."

It is impossible to surpass this song to annexation.

For better or for worse, it is admitted in his latest speech, rectifying
his words of July Fourth that "the current freedom we enjoy is limited."

Very limited indeed, Mr. del Toro.
It is not freedom, it is imperial tolerance.

But immediately afterwards, he intends to justify, no less than the
intention of the current regime, alleging that it must be magnificent because
the writ of habeas corpus has been implemented here and schools are maintained.

Let us consider this statement with coldness.
The school is established as much to build as to destroy.
That is why there is a grave conflict between the different schools,
historical and contemporary conflict.
Empires in all times and in all places plant and
sow schools to disorient, first, and then demolish the
nationalities subject to its force.
That is our case.
The writ of habeas corpus, like any other recourse or provision
of the will that prevails in this country, is revocable at any
moment if the United States Congress so wishes, and can be
suspended by the Governor of the colony or the President of the United States, without even consulting the people of Puerto Rico.
What kind of collective guarantee does that constitute?
Now, Mr. del Toro is disposed, judging by his own
words and without the slightest concern to continue his
annexationist propaganda from his judge's chair.
Nationalism takes up his challenge.
It will not take a step without encountering the most serious opposition to his
harmful path for the country.
Where does Mr. del Toro intend to take the judiciary with his
example?
He owes an answer to the Homeland where he was born, although it, for
now, is powerless to demand accountability.

FACING THE STRUGGLE.
NATIONALISM IS IMMEDIATE ACTION

San Juan de Puerto Rico
July 26, 1930

Lcdo. Luis Vergne Ortiz,
San Juan de Puerto Rico.

My dear friend:

"An orientation!" the politicians of conventionalism cry.
What is an orientation for? To succeed in the elections? That
sole purpose is pursued.
For the patriot there are no doubts or hesitations. The orientation? To
Independence!
Without compromising with the subjection.
Without tolerating the negation of the Homeland.
Nothing is more intolerant than the truth!
Nothing is more intolerable than the negation of the Homeland's personality.
Its negation entails all its evils: collapse of its culture;
foreign political tutelage and by force; economic exploitation and
misery.
Intransigence before the pretensions of an exotic power that
stiffens us. Intransigence before the native henchmen who traffic with their
own personality. They would accept slavery willingly if it
kept them fat.
You are one of the "crazy ones" for the Ideal of Independence!
That decisive decision of character is required to redeem
Puerto Rico. It entails the spirit of heroic sacrifice.
We owe it to ourselves but also to posterity. Let us lift
our people from that miserable condition of beggar to which its
exploiters have reduced it.
Facing the struggle: Nationalism is immediate action. It neither tolerates
nor compromises with the domination of a foreign power.

Yours in the Cause,
P. ALBIZU CAMPOS

PEDRO ALBIZU CAMPOS
ECONOMIC INDEPENDENCE

THE NEW NORTH AMERICAN TARIFF
I
To place its merchandise in Puerto Rico, the Department of
Commerce of the United States maintains its own office in this
capital. In each important city of that country, an
office is maintained by the same Department, but with a
entirely different purpose: it is to teach merchants and industrialists
North Americans how to sell their merchandise in foreign nations.
The office existing in San Juan has never been dedicated to illustrating
our languishing commerce, already almost bankrupt due to
North American competition, how it can intervene in the
international competition in the placement of products from Puerto Rico.
For the Department of Commerce of the United States, Puerto Rico
is a foreign nation. This office provides the necessary data to
North American commerce to destroy our own commerce and the
international competition.
Not long ago the head of this office declared publicly that
90 percent of Puerto Rico's foreign trade was with the United States
States and 10 percent with other foreign nations; and that this 10
percent had to be eliminated so that the foreign trade of
Puerto Rico was exclusively with the United States.
We all know the difficulties faced by the importer of
merchandise not originating from the United States. The procedures are becoming more difficult each time. When it is not the Department of Agriculture or the
of Health that renders the merchandise unusable, any requirement is demanded
unexpected, but there is the consequent delay in delivery, to the serious
detriment of the importer and, he, gradually has to resort to the
North American market to supply the needs of the national
demand.
And all this despite the fact that the North American tariff prevails in Puerto Rico.
But that 10 percent of foreign trade that it also wants

to absorb the United States, has not been able to be eliminated and now
the new tariff has been approved that heavily taxes almost all
foreign merchandise not of North American origin and necessary
for our consumption.

The first effect of the new tariff law imposed on us has
been to raise the price of those goods by the difference resulting
between the types of the old and the new tariff.

Of course, with the import of non-
North American origin, by this insurmountable barrier, and with all the
customs administrative machine to contain any possible entry of
non-Yankee origin, the desire of the United States will soon be satisfied
to have absorbed 100 percent of our foreign trade.

This is the effect of free cabotage. Anyone would believe that Puerto
Rico would derive some benefit. Quite the opposite happens.

The commerce and industry of the United States have an absolute
absolute monopoly of the market of Puerto Rico. Monopoly that exists by virtue
of the tariff law imposed on the country, and also, because the Puerto Rican
nation lacks the powers to defend itself from it. The political
power of the United States is used in our land to hoard
our wealth, be it agrarian, industrial or commercial.

The result is as follows: the North American seller imposes a
special price for Puerto Rico, which is almost always 10 percent,
15 percent and up to 20 percent at times, higher than that prevailing
for the same merchandise in the United States, and even twice as high
than that established for that merchandise destined for other nations. For
that sole concept, Puerto Rico pays an annual tribute to the United States
whose average does not fall below $10,000.00, without taking into account that the
merchandise that is launched on the market of Puerto Rico is of second
or third class, that is, junk.

A notable architect drew our attention to the fact that in Puerto Rico
it was not possible to build a building without ordering the materials directly from the
United States, because first-class merchandise did not come to our commerce from there.
class. Puerto Rico is considered what is called in the
North American commercial jargon junk marcket, that is, market of
junk.

If we take into account that 50 percent of that merchandise
would not be available in the United States or in any foreign market, and
considering that our import rarely falls below
$80,000,000, the fabulous profit and exploitation
that the invading nation derives from our commerce can be clearly seen.

This is the high tribute we pay for its military occupation.

It has been spoken by those who do not know the tariff issues
about the benefits that Puerto Rico can acquire by placing its
own products in the North American market. And, let's talk about
sugar.

Four large North American sugar mills own more than
half of the most fertile lands in the country. The benefits that from
sugar could be derived are almost entirely for the capital
North American. The money that this represents never reaches Puerto Rico.

The profits of these companies are extraordinary because in addition
to the tariff bonus they enjoy, they have the privilege of paying
to the treasury those amounts that suit them. The public power
has never dared to intervene even to determine the real estate that they own, and those they declare are taxed at their pleasure, and they
are charged the contributions they willingly want to pay because the
Attorney General or the Treasurer of the Colony have never dared to
compel them by way of auction in public auction.
At this time nobody knows the millions and millions that these
companies owe the treasury.

As for the *colonos*, if the price of sugar is low, of course
they are ruined; and if the price of sugar is high, they find that
the amount of sucrose in the juice of the cane from their farms has decreased
due to an unknown miracle.

To avoid this fraud against the *colonos*, the legislature of the
colony came up with the idea of appointing a chemist without assistants to
inspect all the laboratories of these octopuses!
We already know what this poor man will do!
There are some native and non-North American nationality interests
who own assets dedicated to the cultivation of cane and factories for the
elaboration of sugar. These benefit from free cabotage.
They constitute, however, a small minority constantly attacked by the treasury
and besieged by the North American sugar or
banking companies that struggle to keep their farms.
The fight, evidently, is very unequal. It is unnecessary to speak of
its consequences.

But this group represents the native and non-
North American interests most disoriented as to the goodness of free cabotage.
They tenaciously oppose a change of political situation, be it to
the constitution of the Republic or the conversion of Puerto Rico into a
North American province, "state", because any of these
political solutions would end the irresponsible regime prevailing,
and they join the invading interests, under attractive banners such as
autonomy to maintain the status quo.

Those are the interests that dominate all the political parties of
Puerto Rico, with the exception of the Nationalist Party.

As for tobacco, everyone knows that most of the capital
invested in that wealth is North American. There remain some
native landowners, but North American buyers
maintain a perpetual conspiracy against them to fix the price of their
product. Thus the value of a quintal of tobacco in the hands of a
Puerto Rican producer is less than the amount that quintal pays to
the customs for import duty. That is to say, that the product in
our hands is worth nothing.

And if we add to this that our tobacco growers and other
native landowners have fallen into the clutches of the Federal
Agricultural Bank that today threatens to execute their farms immediately
because they cannot pay the interest and amortization capital due to
the crisis created by this system of exploitation for thirty years,

the country will clearly see that the native interests will have to submit
unconditionally to the conditions imposed by the
Yankee buyer.
The fruit industry is in similar conditions. 60 percent
of the capital invested is North American: the invaders are the ones who
benefit from the tariff.
The most important industry in the country after sugar is
currently the work of fabrics and handmade seams. The capital,
for the most part, is also North American, the only beneficiary of free
cabotage.
It is our women who are exploited by that industry. In honor of the
Colonial Legislature it must be said that it approved a minimum wage law,
that benefited, directly, all workers, men and
women, but the Supreme Court of the United States declared it null because
being unconstitutional.
Our working women, like men, are subject to the same
vile exploitation of this invading capital, without any political
power to counteract that exploitation.
That is to say, that, not even by concept of salary, does Puerto Rico derive any
benefit from free cabotage. Our working mass is another great mine that
the Yankee has found in its path.
The so-called Socialist Party of the country has never dealt with this
problem, whose solution depends on the political status, but instead of
defending the working masses, it has openly made common cause with the
colonial regime, preaching and defending the "permanent solidarity" between Puerto Rico and the United States.
As this needle industry has not been able to concentrate in a few
hands yet, some Puerto Ricans benefit from free cabotage.
How to annul them?, is the question that the Government
of the colony has formulated.
With the apparently laudable purpose of fixing the identification of
Puerto Rican products in the United States, the colonial government has
approved a factory mark law. Every native product
will have to bear the mark designated by the government so that its origin is not
can be put in doubt, and that origin is established only by that mark.
Here we see the centralization of this industry in Yankee hands.
This measure in the hands of a government of good faith would serve to lift
our industry, but in the hands of a government of bad faith like the one
that exists in Puerto Rico, official guarantee will be denied to whoever is not
graceful to that government or to the interests that that government wants to
impose in our country.
It is clear that, when the United States absorbs all our foreign commerce,
the imports from other foreign nations will cease and
the customs revenues that today amount to the
respectable sum of almost three million dollars.
To the autonomists of good faith we draw attention to this
consequence of free cabotage between the United States and Puerto Rico,
which would bring, as a consequence, the loss of fiscal revenues
important, as it would result if Puerto Rico were annexed as

North American province, "state", thus suffering all the damages
of that political status, without any of the benefits that,
constitutionally accompany it.
In our next article we will analyze the purposes that
inform the law creating the Bureau of Industry and Commerce.
In summary, with free cabotage, and a tariff that excludes all
foreign competition, our agrarian, industrial and
commercial wealth is at the mercy of the strongest economic power in the world, which,
in addition to its intrinsic financial strength, has retained in its hands
all political powers in our country, which will remain powerless to this
invasion unless there is a reaction of intense nationalism that
can restrain it.

TARIFF AUTONOMY
1
The dominant interests, mostly North American, are
opposed to the definition of the political status of the country and defend by all
the means the irresponsible system of government that prevails, alleging
several pretexts, but at bottom the opposition of this invisible power
is reduced to reasons of an economic nature.
If the country is merged into a North American province, "state", the
absolute dominion that they have today of the organisms and officials ceases
colonials. The North American province "state", would have legal power,
in abstract legal theory to suppress absenteeism and
latifundism, and could end the abuse of imposing the fiscal burden
on natives and small landowners, to force them to sell their
farms or cede their businesses to invading North American interests, which
enjoy the privilege of paying to the treasury what suits them and when
it suits them.
We assume that that power is abstract because we know the force
of the North American plutocracy and how it exploits its own
fellow citizens on the continent. Whoever wants to see pictures of misery
move to New York, to Boston or to any other city
North American. Our working class can expect nothing from the "state",
that is not the continuation of the present exploitation.
The political difficulties to extinguish Puerto Rico in a province
North American "state", are insurmountable, both from the point of
North American point of view as from ours.
We are not interested in what North Americans think about the "state" solution.
"state". It is their privilege to determine who can be part of
the Yankee family. As for us, suffice it to say that the
Puerto Ricans are not willing to renounce our inalienable right
to constitute ourselves in a free Homeland, at no time or for
any reason.
To merge our nationality into the North American unit, be it this
the latter, good or bad, is not something that can be seriously considered by
those who have a clear concept of what nationality is. Its
existence defines our spiritual life oriented spontaneously
towards the superior civilization to which we belong, and is the only guarantee
of the material well-being of our people.
Only our Republic can feel the Homeland's needs, and
remedy them with the superior interest of making itself worthy of occupying a position of
vanguard in contemporary civilization.
These interests know well the impossibility of the conversion of
our Homeland into a North American province, "state". They oppose the
propaganda that leads to that anticipating the possible evil of losing the

monopoly of the reigning political and material power, because all
discussion of the political status necessarily leads to exposing the
existing privileges and outrages, and that is taking away sleep from those who
enrich themselves based on the systematic exploitation of our natural and human resources.
By more conceited than a monopoly feels, even if it is supported by the
North American force that currently guarantees the exploitation of our Homeland,
it loses all tranquility upon receiving the *internazo* of analytical criticism,
especially when the system that protects it has almost put it in possession
of all the Puerto Rican wealth.

And that analysis is inevitable when discussing the political status. It
is significant that the directors of the pro-North American province movement,
"state", have launched to discredit their own
program, propagating the false claim that the province "state",
could not be sustained economically, appealing to the sordidness of those
who have something to lose.
Of course, nationalism repudiates this political solution that
it considers suicidal, but always sheltered in the truth, it cannot stop
rejecting an argument without any basis, and that serves only to
increase the disorientation that disables the country from leaving the irresponsible
colonialism, which is the true purpose pursued by those who launch such
species.
It is to be expected that those dominant interests will combat the
National Independence.
In this case the intention of maintaining the colonialism becomes
absolutely manifest.
The country from which they extract enormous wealth annually is presented
poor and worthy of North American charity, which, by the way,
has never disembarked in our land. It appears embroiled without
possibility of redeeming itself. It is intended to make it depend economically
exclusively on the North American market, and as a corollary, they point out
that free cabotage would cease by imposing own tariffs thus provoking
North American reciprocity, which would determine the ruin of the
country.
We have accused the government of the colony of tolerating the
concealment of assets in the possession of large
North American companies, of taxing their assets at low rates, and of allowing them
to pay the contributions they want and when it
is convenient to their interests.

The proportion between the volume of trade of a country and the value

The total foreign trade of the United States, exports and
imports, amounts to the global sum of ten billion dollars
and the total property amounts to three hundred and eighty billion
dollars. The proportion is one to thirty-eight (1:38).
The foreign trade of Puerto Rico gives a total of one hundred and seventy-five
five million and the taxation of the property rises only to about
three hundred and fifty million. The proportion of one to two (1:2) And
