Comes your letter of adhesion to our party published
recently in "La Correspondencia". You have true orientation.
Your letter rejects that wave of submission.
You leave behind Unionist liberalism. We salute, with
enthusiasm, when that effort of renovation began. We long
that the day arrive when all political parties consign in their
program as sole longing the Independence of their Homeland.
How not to rejoice that that party shield itself to the exclusive
ideal of Independence and prepare to adopt the Nationalist
tactic of immediate action against the usurpers of our
right to the life of Free Nation?
But we have seen, as you, that a tremendous defeat is all
that awaits that Unionist youth. It is certain that some prominent
directors offer their adhesion, but the majority of the influential
are decisively against it. There are those who hasten to show solidarity in
these very moments with the most unconditional existing.
Faced with the supreme right of Independence, one hears, at this
stage, the refrain of autonomy.
Is an autonomous regime possible under the flag of
United States? Its constitution ignores such a political solution.
Either Puerto Rico opts for its inalienable right to Independence or
it voluntarily extinguishes itself in a Yankee province, "state", or
it will indefinitely suffer the present territorial regime of absolute
irresponsibility before the governed.
No autonomous regime can be compared with
Independence, but there are those who still accept in advance and
voluntarily, the restrictions that are only tolerated when force
imposes them.
There is no Nationalism outside of the Nationalist Party.
To its ranks those who love the Independence of the Homeland!
Let us pressure the enemies of our liberation to form their
annexationist bloc so that they alone bear the historical responsibility
of having done everything in their power to end the
personality of their own country, and thus ingratiate themselves with an invader
who, while using them, feels profound contempt for them,

Yours in the cause,

Pedro ALBIZU CAMPOS.

THE JUDICIARY AND ANNEXATIONISM³¹
By Pedro Albizu Campos
To the general censure against his speech of July Fourth, Mr.
Emilio del Toro Cuevas, ratifies himself in his annexationist political creed, and
reaffirms his intention not to resign his position as Judge before
intervening in political contests. That is the synthesis of his
latest speech delivered at the Ateneo.
And this is a challenge to the sensible opinion of the country. We congratulate him for his
courage in undressing a situation understood only by a limited
number of people.
The Judiciary in every Government is the watchdog of the regime,
be it liberal or reactionary, responsible or irresponsible to those
subject to its direction.
Puerto Rico suffers an absolutely irresponsible regime. The
legislative initiative, in addition to being restricted, beforehand, must
face the irrevocable veto of the exotic power; and, if its
initiatives come to become laws, these can be amended or
totally revoked at any time by the Congress of United
States, the executive is openly the invader in person; the Judiciary
has hints of criollism in its composition, and, among the courts there is
one that is called the Supreme Court of Puerto Rico, name that
implies judicial sovereignty, but the legal reality is another.
It is not a court of Puerto Rico, because it is the president of
United States of America, who, with the advice and
consent of the Senate of that country, appoints its judges, but as
Puerto Ricans are obliged to pay the expenses of the government
that has been imposed on us, the budget of said court including the
salaries of its judges, has to be covered by our treasury.
Nor is it supreme in anything, because its judgments are subject to
reconsideration on appeal before two superior foreign courts,
that is, before the Circuit Court of Boston and the Supreme Court of
United States of America.
In short, it is a North American territorial court imposed on
our country by the power that holds its sovereignty.
The Judiciary, in an irresponsible system of government, has to
be extremely careful in its defense of the high policy of the regime.
It cannot allow any precedent to be set that undermines the status
quo.
And this is more delicate where the invader intends the definitive annexation
of the country dominated by force as happens in Puerto Rico.

Also, as part of the high policy to follow, it has to take into
account, very seriously, despite the invading force that may
support it, the opinion of the subjected nation, which cannot lose faith in
the administration of justice.

We understand that it is difficult for a Puerto Rican to hold a
position in the judiciary if he meditates on the responsibility he incurs when
accepting the position.

Terrible dilemma assaults him; how to unconditionally support the
regime and at the same time be loyal to his homeland of origin.

For that reason, those compatriots who have not communed with the plan
of North American absorption and who have wished to see their country free from
foreign interventions, have resigned their judicial positions to
dedicate themselves to defending the cause of Independence; likewise, many
annexationists in the same situation, have submitted their resignation before
launching themselves to preach statehood, thus respecting the opinion of the country, which
expects of its citizens, that, given the difficulty of administering justice
in an irresponsible regime, obstacles should not be created for the exercise
of that ministry by the political propaganda of judges or judicial officials
during the exercise of their positions.

This principle has been imposed and no transgressor has escaped public
disapproval for his ill-considered action contrary to it.

In general, the judiciary that this imperial regime has imposed on us
has adhered to this principle of elevated ethics despite the serious
official pressure that has been exerted on it.

This public recognition we all owe, despite the
absurd and unpatriotic actions of some judges and judicial officials.

We esteemed Mr. Emilio Del Toro as a man of sufficient
equanimity and character, who, despite his annexationist ideas,
would never be the person who would intend to break with this severe norm.

But, to the general reprimand for his unfortunate speech of July
Fourth, he has answered with a challenge full of pride to the Puerto Rican nation.

Here are his words: "My judicial positions have not been nor will be obstacles to expound my ideas."

He awaits with fervor "the complete freedom that we will enjoy in no
distant day, when the star of Borinquen shines resplendent in the
American constellation as the symbol of one more state in the bosom of
the union".

It is impossible to surpass this song to annexation.

For better or worse, it is admitted in his last speech, rectifying his
words of July Fourth that "the current freedom that we enjoy is limited."

Very limited indeed, Mr. del Toro.

It is not freedom, it is imperial tolerance.

But immediately afterwards, he intends to justify, nothing less than
the intention of the current regime, alleging that it must be magnificent because
the remedy of habeas corpus has been implanted here and schools are maintained.

Let us consider this assertion with coldness.
The school, is established equally to construct as to destroy.
That is the reason for the grave conflict between the different schools,
historical and contemporary conflict.
The empires in all times and in all places plant and
sow schools to disorient, first, and then demolish the
nationalities subject to their force.
That is our case.
The remedy of habeas corpus, like every other remedy or disposition
of the will that prevails in this country, is revocable at any
moment if the Congress of United States so pleases and can be
suspended by the Governor of the colony or the President of United
States, without even consulting the people of Puerto Rico.
What kind of collective guarantee does that constitute?
Now then, Mr. del Toro prepares, to judge by his own
words and without the slightest concern to continue his
annexationist propaganda from his judge's chair.
Nationalism takes up his challenge.
It will not take a step without stumbling upon the most serious opposition to his
disastrous trajectory for the country.
Where does Mr. del Toro intend to take the judiciary with his
example?
He owes an answer to the homeland where he was born, although it, for
now, is unarmed to demand responsibilities from him.

Without tolerating the negation of the Homeland.

Intransigence before the pretensions of an exotic power, that grips us.

ECONOMIC INDEPENDENCE³³

THE NEW NORTH AMERICAN TARIFF
I
To place its merchandise in Puerto Rico, the Department of
Commerce of United States maintains its own office in this
capital. In each important city of that country an
office is maintained by the same Department, but with a
entirely different purpose: it is to teach the merchants and
North American industrialists how to sell their merchandise in foreign nations.
The office existing in San Juan has never dedicated itself to illustrating
our languishing commerce, already almost bankrupt due to the
North American competition, how it can intervene in the international competition
in the placement of products of Puerto Rico.
For the Department of Commerce of United States, Puerto Rico
is a foreign nation. This office supplies the necessary data to
North American commerce to destroy our own commerce and
international competition.
Not long ago the head of this office declared publicly that
90 percent of the foreign commerce of Puerto Rico was with United
States and 10 percent with other foreign nations; and that this 10
percent had to be eliminated so that the foreign commerce of
Puerto Rico would be exclusively with United States.
We all know the difficulties that the importer of
merchandise not originating from United States encounters. The procedures are increasingly
more difficult. When it is not the Department of Agriculture or the
Department of Health that renders the merchandise useless, any
unexpected requirement is demanded, but there comes the consequent delay in the delivery, to the grave
detriment of the importer and, the latter, gradually has to resort to the
North American market to supply the needs of the national demand.
And all this despite the North American tariff
ruling in Puerto Rico. But that 10 percent of foreign commerce that desires

to absorb United States as well, has not been able to be eliminated and now
the new tariff has been approved that heavily taxes almost all
foreign merchandise of non-North American origin and necessary
for our consumption.
The first effect of the new tariff law imposed on us has
been to raise the price of that merchandise by the difference resulting
between the rates of the old and the new tariff.

Of course, hindered the import of non-
North American origin, by this insurmountable barrier, and with all the
customs administrative machine to contain any possible entry of
non-Yankee origin, the desire of United States will soon be satisfied
of having absorbed 100 percent of our foreign commerce.
This is the effect of free cabotage. Anyone would believe that Puerto
Rico would have to derive some benefit. Everything happens the opposite.
The commerce and the industry of United States have an absolute
monopoly of the market of Puerto Rico. Monopoly that exists by virtue
of the tariff law imposed on the country, and also, because the
Puerto Rican nation lacks powers to defend itself from it. The
political power of United States is used in our land to monopolize
our wealth be it agrarian, industrial or commercial.
The result is the following: the North American seller imposes
special price for Puerto Rico, which is almost always 10 percent,
15 percent and up to 20 percent at times, higher than the one that rules
for the same merchandise in United States, and up to twice as high
than the one established for that merchandise destined to other nations. By
that sole concept, Puerto Rico pays an annual tribute to United States
whose average does not fall below $10,000.00, without taking into account that the
merchandise that is launched on the market of Puerto Rico is of second
or third class, that is, junk.
A notable architect called our attention to the fact that in Puerto Rico
one could not build a building without ordering the materials directly from
United States, because from there merchandise of first class does not come to our commerce.
Puerto Rico is considered what is called in the
North American commercial jargon junk market, that is, junk market.

If we take into account that 50 percent of that merchandise would
not be available in United States nor in any foreign market, and
considering that our importation rarely descends to
$80,000,000 can be seen clearly the fabulous gain and exploitation
that the invading nation derives from our commerce.
This is the high tribute that we pay for its military occupation.
There has been talk by those who do not know the tariff issues
about the benefits that Puerto Rico can acquire when placing its
own products in the North American market. And, let us talk about
sugar.
Four large North American sugar centrals possess more than
half of the most fertile lands of the country. The benefits that from
sugar could be derived are almost totally for the North American capital.
The money that that represents never arrives to Puerto Rico.

The gains of these companies are extraordinary because in addition
to the tariff bonus that they enjoy they have the privilege of paying
to the treasury those amounts that suit them. The public power
has never dared to intervene even to determine the real
properties that they possess, and the ones that they declare are valued at their pleasure, and they are charged the contributions that they want to pay because the
Attorney General or the Treasurer of the Colony have never dared to
press them by way of auction in public auction.
At this hour no one knows the millions and millions that these
companies owe to the treasury.

As for the *colonos*, if the price of sugar is low, of course
they are ruined; and if the price of sugar is high, they find that
the amount of sucrose in the juice of the cane of their farms has decreased
as a result of an unknown miracle.
To avoid this fraud against the *colonos*, the legislature of the
colony came up with the idea of appointing a chemist without assistants to
inspect all the laboratories of these octopuses!
We already know what this poor man will do!
There are some native interests and of non-North American nationality
that possess properties dedicated to the cultivation of cane and factories for the
elaboration of sugar. These benefit from free cabotage.
They constitute, however, a small minority attacked by the treasury
constantly and besieged by the North American sugar or
banking octopuses that fight to keep their farms.
The struggle, evidently, is very unequal. It is unnecessary to speak of
its consequences.

But this group represents the native and non-
North American interests more disoriented as to the goodness of free cabotage.
They oppose tenaciously a change of political situation, be it the
constitution of the Republic or the conversion of Puerto Rico into a
North American province, "state", because any of these
political solutions would end the irresponsible regime prevailing,
and they unite with the invading interests, under attractive little flags like
autonomy to maintain the status quo.

Those are the interests that dominate all the political parties of
Puerto Rico, with the exception of the Nationalist Party.
As for tobacco, everyone knows that the greater part of the capital
invested in that wealth is North American. There remain some
native landowners, but the North American buyers
maintain a perpetual conspiracy against them to fix the price of their
product. Thus the value of a quintal of tobacco in the hands of a
Puerto Rican producer is less than the amount that that quintal pays
to customs for import duties. That is to say, that the product in
our hands is worth nothing.

And if to this we add that our tobacconists and other
native landowners have fallen into the clutches of the Federal
Agricultural Bank that today threatens them with executing their farms immediately
because they cannot pay the interest and amortization capital due to
the crisis created by this system of exploitation during thirty years,

the country will see clearly that the native interests will have to submit
unconditionally to the conditions that the Yankee buyer imposes on them.
The fruit industry is in similar conditions. 60 percent
of the capital invested is North American: it is the invaders who benefit from the tariff.
The most important industry of the country after sugar is
currently the works of fabrics and hand-made seams. The capital,
in its greater part, is also North American, sole beneficiary of
free cabotage.
It is our women who are exploited by that industry. In honor of the
Colonial Legislature it must be said that it approved a minimum wage law,
that benefited, directly, all the workers, men and
women, but the Supreme Court of United States declared it null for
being unconstitutional.
Our working women like the men are subject to the same
vile exploitation of this invading capital, without there being any political power
to counteract that exploitation.
That is to say, that, not even by concept of salary, does Puerto Rico derive, any
benefit from free cabotage. Our working mass is another great mine that
the Yankee has found in his path.
The so-called Socialist Party of the country, has never dealt with this
problem, whose solution depends on the political status, but instead of
defending the working masses has made itself openly in solidarity with the
colonial regime, preaching and defending the "permanent solidarity"
between Puerto Rico and United States.
As this needle industry has not been able to concentrate in few
hands yet, some Puerto Ricans benefit from free cabotage.
How to annul them?, is the question that the Government
of the colony has formulated.
With the apparently laudable purpose of fixing the identification of the
Puerto Rican products in United States, the colonial government has
had a factory brand law approved. Every native product
will have to carry the brand that the government designates so that its origin cannot be doubted, and that origin is established only by that brand.
We see here the centralization in Yankee hands of this industry.
This measure in the hands of a government of good faith would serve to raise
our industry, but in the hands of a government of bad faith like the one
existing in Puerto Rico, the official guarantee will be denied to whoever is not
agreeable to that government or to the interests that that government wants to
impose in our country.
It is clear that, when United States absorbs all our foreign commerce,
the imports from other foreign nations will cease and the
customs entries that today amount to the
respectable sum of almost three million dollars will totally disappear.
To the autonomists of good faith we call attention to this
consequence of free cabotage between United States and Puerto Rico,
that would bring, as a consequence, the loss of
important fiscal entries, as it would result if Puerto Rico were annexed as

North American province, "state", thus suffering all the damages
of that political status, without any of the benefits that,
constitutionally accompany it.

In our next article we will analyze the purposes that
inform the law creating the Office of Industry and Commerce.

In summary, with free cabotage, and a tariff that excludes all
foreign competition, our agrarian, industrial and
commercial wealth is at the mercy of the strongest economic power in the world, which,
in addition to its intrinsic financial strength, has retained in its hands
all the political powers in our country, that, will continue unarmed to this
invasion unless there is a reaction of intense nationalism that
can curb it.

TARIFF AUTONOMY

1

The dominant interests, in their majority North American, oppose
the definition of the political status of the country and defend by all
means the system of irresponsible government that prevails, alleging
various pretexts, but in the background the opposition of this invisible power is
reduced to reasons of economic character.
If the country is melted into a North American province, "state", ceases
the absolute dominion that they have today of the colonial organisms and officials.
The North American province "state", would have legal power,
in abstract legal theory to suppress absenteeism and
latifundismo, and could end the abuse of imposing the fiscal burden
on the natives and small proprietors, to oblige them to sell their
farms or cede their businesses to invading North American interests, that
enjoy the privilege of paying to the treasury what suits them and when
it suits them.
We assume that that power is abstract because we know the strength
of the North American plutocracy and how it exploits its own
fellow citizens on the continent. He who wants to see pictures of misery
to transfer to New York, to Boston or to any other North American
city. Our working class can expect nothing from the "state",
that is not the continuation of the present exploitation.
The political difficulties to extinguish Puerto Rico in North American
province "state", are insurmountable, both from the
North American point of view as from ours.
We are not interested in what the North Americans opine about the solution
"state". It is their privilege to determine who can form part of
the Yankee family. As for us, suffice it to say that the
Puerto Ricans are not willing to renounce our inalienable right
to constitute ourselves into a free Homeland, at any time nor for
any reason.
To melt our nationality in the North American unity, be this
latter, good or bad, is not something that can be seriously considered by
those who have a clear concept of what nationality is. Its
existence defines our spiritual life oriented spontaneously
towards the superior civilization to which we belong, and is the only guarantee
of the material well-being of our people.
Only our Republic can feel the homeland necessities, and
remedy them with the superior interest of making itself worthy of occupying a position
of vanguard in contemporary civilization.
