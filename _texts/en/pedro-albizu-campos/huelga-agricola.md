The Agricultural Strike
By Pedro Albizu Campos
The agricultural strike is ongoing throughout the East, and in the South,
up to Ponce. If Mayagüez rises up today, as is to be expected, this
national movement will cover and paralyze the activities of all
the sugar mills.
The workers have organized themselves into a corporation to have
legal personality, and thus prevent anyone from daring to speak on
their behalf. Their headquarters are located in Guayama because
there, more than six thousand working men, in the most
imposing assembly that can be conceived, spoke and acted for themselves
the working masses, which in reality constitute around
ninety percent of the total population of the country. He is the true
people of Puerto Rico who manifest themselves with all serenity and valor.
The workers had organized at the end of the
activities of last Sunday four sections of the Asociación
de Trabajadores de Puerto Rico: Guayama, Fajardo, Luquillo and Ca-
nóvanas. On Monday, the organization of the entire nation will continue with the fervor that this noble cause has. The assemblies held
in the aforementioned cities have been acts of pure patriotism and high
culture, revealing the fact observed by the wise Humboldt in his
visit to our lands a century ago, that the Puerto Rican nation
constitutes the most intelligent society in the New World.
Our people have demonstrated that they can resolve in assembly,
in the public square itself, with wisdom and tranquility, and valor,
any matter that affects their lives.
It is strange that the workers have not organized until
now. Lawyers, doctors, school teachers, merchants,
sailors, professionals, sugar growers, business employees,
employees of the insular government, chemists, pharmacists, indus-

trials, tenants, that is, all professions and activities of
social scope, are organized in the corporate form, with
rights and legal responsibility.
The workers already constitute the strongest corporation that
can be formed because they are the true power and the true
source of wealth that the Homeland has.
Each Section of the Asociación de Trabajadores consists of
a president and a secretary, and a member for each class of agricultural task, and has to be a bona fide worker, of the absolute con-
fidence of his comrades, that is, a natural director in the
corresponding agricultural specialty. As the specialties in the
sugarcane field are around twelve, the board of directors of the
section amounts to fifteen men.
Only the specialist in a certain class of work can
understand the hardship of the work in their respective field and
determine the needs that the men who
dedicate themselves to that specialty have to cover. Thus the carpenters, bricklayers,
mechanics, cane cutters, "zañeros" etc., will have a member of their
midst who represents their specialty in the respective section of the
"Asociación de Trabajadores de Puerto Rico". The harm suffered by the
worker is due in large part to the disorganization of their
hosts. But this harm will be cut off at the root, with which
the apocryphal leadership that has exploited the
great working mass until now will disappear. It will also end the barbaric exploitation
to which the worker has been subjected at the hands of the great
employer interests without human sense.
The workers have rejected the proposals formulated
by the sugar corporations for very serious reasons that
are of interest to the nation.
We extract from the list of workers' demands these reasons:
"1. Because salaries are established lower than those already
existing.
"2. Because the 12-hour day is maintained for the
factories, a day that is inhuman.
"3. Because the supposed participation of the
workers in sugar profits in case of a sugar price increase is a myth.
Because the price of the product is already stabilized.
"4. Because the sugar growers are earning a fabulous
sum of money at the expense of the low wages of the workers and

also because they enjoy the tariff bonus in the North American market, of $2.00 per quintal. If the sugar growers who have
interests in Cuba, earn money selling their sugar in the North American market without counting on this bonus of $2.00 per quintal,
